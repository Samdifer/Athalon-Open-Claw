## Background

Renata Vasquez grew up in Corpus Christi, three miles from the airport, and spent her adolescence watching the Navy T-34s and T-44s fly patterns at NAS Corpus Christi before the flight training mission moved out. Her uncle worked line operations at the old Naval Air Depot; she didn't understand exactly what he did, but she understood that aviation maintenance was work you could be serious about. She enrolled in A&P school at Del Mar College at 19 and had her certificate at 21.

Her first job was at a small FBO doing Cessna 172 and Piper Cherokee maintenance — the standard proving ground. Within two years she was being handed the structural write-ups because she was methodical and she wasn't afraid of the SRM. At 25, she was hired by a heavy MRO operator in San Antonio running 737 line maintenance and heavy checks, where she spent nine years in the structural shop doing skin repairs, corrosion mapping, and major inspection structural work on 737 Classics and early NGs. It was there that she submitted her first Form 337, then her twentieth, then her hundredth. The 737 world generates a steady stream of major structural repairs — fuselage skin panels, stringer repairs, frame repairs — and the Form 337 paperwork chain is not optional. She knows the form the way a tax accountant knows a 1040.

She also learned, in those nine years, that the documentation chain around a major structural repair is not a bureaucratic formality. It is the record that tells the next engineer — who might be looking at this airframe in three years, or fifteen, or thirty — what happened here, why, and what was done about it. She has pulled aircraft records on incoming King Airs at Gulf Coast and found major structural repairs with no Form 337 in the aircraft's permanent file. Every time it happens, she files a discrepancy report and contacts the prior operator. Every time, the story is the same: the 337 was done on paper, the paper got separated from the aircraft logbook during a sale, and now nobody can reconstruct the approval chain for a structural modification that is still on the airplane. She is not the kind of person who accepts that as a normal outcome.

She moved back to Corpus Christi in 2017 when Gulf Coast Structural offered her a lead structural tech position. The shop does King Air and Citation structural work, some 737 field repair contract work, and occasional calls on 767 structurals when a regional airline needs a specialist on-site. She has her IA and uses it regularly — primarily for structural RTS sign-offs on major repairs and for annuals on owner-flown King Airs.

She is the person the DOM calls when a structural write-up needs to hold up to FAA scrutiny. She has been through four Certificate Management Office audits at two different shops. Her records have never been a finding. She attributes this entirely to the fact that she treats every Form 337 and every maintenance record as if an FSDO investigator will read it next week. They usually won't. Sometimes they will. She does not adjust her behavior based on the probability of being audited.

She agreed to the Athelon embed when the DOM forwarded the opportunity notice. She had one question before she said yes: does Athelon have a Form 337 reference field in the major repair workflow? She was told it had been added in v2.1. She said she'd come take a look.

## How She Actually Does Structural Work

Renata's workflow is built around a chain of decisions, each of which has a documentation trigger. She thinks of the work order as the spine of that chain — everything else hangs off it. The sequence matters and she doesn't compress it.

**Step 1 — Damage characterization.** Before she touches the aircraft, she opens a work order and enters the damage description in the format she's been using for twelve years: location (station, stringer, frame, rib, or equivalent per structural drawing), type (crack, corrosion, dent, abrasion, impact, disbond), dimensions (length × width × depth), and proximity to primary structure (distance to nearest fastener, seam, or edge). This is not optional documentation she'll get to later. If she opens a work order and the damage description says "skin damage aft fuselage," she writes her own before she opens a panel. She has refused to start work on jobs where she couldn't establish a documented damage baseline.

**Step 2 — SRM disposition.** Is the damage within allowable limits per the applicable SRM, or does it require repair? If repair, is it covered by the SRM? Every structural decision traces to a specific manual, a specific revision, a specific chapter, section, subject, and figure. Not "the SRM." The specific revision of the SRM that applies to this serial number, accounting for any Supplemental Type Certificates that alter structural allowables. She has argued with DERs who cited the wrong SRM revision. She was right both times. One of those arguments involved a King Air fuselage stringer that was borderline on the allowable damage chart — the difference between the old revision and the current one was whether it was in-limits or required a repair. The revision matters.

**Step 3 — Repair classification.** Is this a minor repair or a major repair under 14 CFR Part 43 Appendix A? This is not a judgment call she makes loosely. The criteria are specific: primary structural members, repairs that affect the structural strength or flight characteristics of the aircraft, repairs to pressurized structure, repairs involving engineering design data not provided in the Type Certificate. She has encountered shops where the classification defaulted to "minor" unless someone specifically escalated it. At her shops, the classification is made affirmatively — she documents why something is minor as often as she documents why it's major.

**Step 4 — The 337 chain.** When a major repair classification is established, the work order is immediately flagged. The Form 337 is prepared in parallel with the repair, not after. She does not start a major repair without the 337 framework in place because she has seen what happens when you do: the repair gets done, the aircraft sits on the ramp waiting to go, the customer calls, and someone decides the paperwork can be filed retroactively. It usually gets filed. Sometimes it doesn't. When it doesn't, the aircraft flies with an unrecorded major repair until someone notices. She doesn't work in shops that tolerate this, and she doesn't contribute to it herself.

**Step 5 — DER involvement, when required.** Repairs that exceed SRM coverage, or that involve primary structure without approved repair data, go to a DER. Alejandro Reyes is her primary — they've worked together long enough that she can send him a damage photo and a dimensional sketch and he'll tell her within an hour whether he can write a disposition or whether it needs a full engineering analysis. When Alejandro signs off a repair, his FAA authorization number, the document reference, and any approval conditions go into the maintenance record. This is not optional.

**Step 6 — RTS.** She doesn't sign the return to service until every prior step is documented and she can see the complete chain. Aircraft N-number and serial number. Work order number. Damage description. SRM reference. Repair classification. Form 337 reference number and the name of who prepared the 337. DER reference if applicable. Everything on one screen. If anything is missing, she asks for it. She has held aircraft for two days waiting for a 337 to be properly filed. The customer was unhappy. The aircraft was correctly documented.

## Paper vs. Software History

Renata has used five distinct MRO systems over her career, ranging from a functioning regional standard to a product that will permanently affect how she evaluates software.

**System 1 — CAMP Systems (early career FBO, ~2003–2005):** Used primarily for scheduled maintenance tracking. She was a junior tech interacting with it only tangentially — squawks, task sign-offs, parts requests. No structural documentation workflow relevant at that career stage. No complaints.

**System 2 — MRO Pro (San Antonio heavy MRO, 2004–2013):** Her primary system for nine years. MRO Pro had a "repair type" dropdown with values including "major repair" and "minor repair." This was encouraging. What it did not have was any enforcement downstream of that selection. Selecting "major repair" changed nothing about the work order workflow. There was no Form 337 reference field anywhere in the system. The DOM at that shop solved this by requiring a paper Form 337 copy to be physically attached to every major repair work order jacket before the job could be billed out. The folder left the shop. The software record said nothing. If you searched the MRO Pro database for all major structural repairs on a given tail number, you found work orders with no indication of whether a 337 had been filed. The information lived in physical folders in filing cabinets in the DOM's office, organized by aircraft N-number. For the nine years she was there, this worked because the DOM was meticulous. When the DOM left the company in 2012, it stopped working. The filing system degraded. She watched it happen in her last year there and it was one of the reasons she left.

**System 3 — AvMaint Direct (San Antonio, 2012–2013):** The shop trialed AvMaint Direct for eight months before abandoning it. AvMaint Direct had no repair type classification at all on work orders. "Major repair" and "minor repair" were not concepts the software recognized. There was no Form 337 field. There was no DER reference field. There was no way to link any regulatory document to a work order. She described the experience in her intake interview without emotion, in the flat tone she uses when something has already been processed and filed: *"It was designed by someone who had never encountered an FAA Form 337 and possibly didn't know they existed. I don't mean that to be harsh. I mean that they literally did not account for the regulatory structure that governs what we do. For structural work, it was useless. For anything requiring compliance documentation, it was worse than paper because paper at least has room to write."* She spent those eight months keeping a parallel paper log of all her structural work. When the shop abandoned AvMaint Direct, she was not surprised. She also understood that those eight months of work orders existed in a system with no 337 tracking and no classification records. The repairs were documented — she documented them herself, on paper — but the software record of that period was structurally incomplete in a way that could not be reconstructed.

**System 4 — Corridor (San Antonio, 2013):** Used only for the last few months before she returned to Corpus Christi. Corridor had meaningfully better structure than MRO Pro: "major repair" classification triggered a DOM review flag, which was genuine progress. There was still no dedicated 337 reference field, but the flag at least ensured the DOM touched every major repair record before it closed. The 337 documentation still lived outside the system, but the human check was enforced. She considers Corridor the first system she used that showed evidence of someone understanding the compliance picture.

**System 5 — WingSpan MRO (Gulf Coast Structural, 2017–present):** The most complete structural documentation workflow she'd encountered before Athelon. WingSpan has a repair type field, an "EO/DER reference" field, and — added in the 2021 update — a field labeled "FAA Form 337 Ref #." Real progress. The problem: the field exists but the system does not block RTS authorization when the field is empty on a major repair work order. It is advisory. A tech can authorize RTS, the system will proceed, and the 337 reference field will remain blank in the permanent record. She has seen this happen — not at Gulf Coast Structural, where the shop culture and her own presence create a floor, but at another WingSpan shop whose aircraft come through for follow-on work. Two aircraft in three years arrived with major repair work orders showing empty 337 fields. Both were compliance findings. Both required retroactive paperwork and FSDO notification. Both were caused entirely by the fact that WingSpan treated the 337 reference as optional at the enforcement point. *"An advisory field on a mandatory document is not a field,"* she says. *"It's a suggestion. We have enough suggestions in aviation. We need more enforcement."*

## The Form 337 — Her Requirements, In Her Words

Asked to describe exactly what she needs from any MRO system handling major structural repair documentation, Renata's answer is specific and does not waver:

> "When I classify a work order as major repair — or when the system prompts for that classification, which it should do if I select a repair type that falls under Part 43 Appendix A — the system needs to know from that moment forward that this work order does not close without a Form 337 reference. Not a warning. A block. The RTS authorization button should be grayed out. The sign-off screen should say: 'Form 337 Reference Required.' Not in small print. Not as a checkbox I can check without entering a number. There should be a field, and it should require a value, and until that value is entered, nobody signs the RTS."

On the question of what happens when the 337 hasn't been submitted yet at the time of RTS — a real scenario in field operations:

> "That situation actually happens. The repair is done. The 337 is prepared. In some shops the IA signs the 337 before RTS; in some shops the 337 goes to the DER for co-signature first, which takes time. What I need is the ability to enter a preliminary 337 identifier — our internal tracking number if the FAA document number isn't assigned yet — and have the system record the date and the person who prepared the form. Then when the FAA submission is confirmed, update the field with the final 337 document number. What I cannot have is the RTS signed with the field empty because we're 'waiting for the DER.' That's the moment where shortcuts happen. Something real has to be in that field before RTS. Even if it's our internal tracking number. The field cannot be empty."

On the question of what the RTS sign-off screen must show:

> "Before I put my IA certificate number on a return to service for a major structural repair, I need to see: aircraft N-number and serial number, work order number, work description, repair classification stated explicitly as major repair, Form 337 reference number, and the name of the person who prepared the 337. All of that on one screen before I sign. I want to be looking at the complete chain. If any element is missing, I'm not signing. The software should make that an impossible situation rather than a choice I have to make."

## Learning That Athelon Has the Field — But No UI Workflow Yet

When Renata was briefed on Athelon's schema — specifically that a `form_337_ref` field was added in v2.1 to the `major_repair` work order type, marked as required before RTS authorization — her first reaction was a question, not enthusiasm:

> "Who enforces it? The database layer? The UI? Because if the field is required in the schema but the UI lets you submit without it, the schema requirement means nothing to the people filling out the forms. Every AvMaint Direct field was technically 'required' in some configuration document somewhere. The screen let you tab past all of them."

When told that the enforcement logic exists in the RTS authorization flow — that the `rts_authorized` action on a `major_repair` work order returns a validation error if `form_337_ref` is null — she paused for approximately five seconds.

> "Okay. That's the right answer. That's where the block should be."

Then the follow-up: the UI does not yet have a dedicated workflow for the Form 337 reference field. There is no prompt when a work order is classified as major repair. There is no visual indicator on the work order card that the field is missing or required. The input field is not surfaced in the standard work order edit view. The `form_337_ref` field exists in the schema and the validation exists in the RTS authorization backend, but the path to populate the field currently requires raw work order editing or direct API access.

Her response was measured and exact:

> "So the right architecture is there but the interface doesn't surface it, and a tech in the field has no idea the field exists unless someone tells them. Which means the first time they try to authorize RTS on a major repair, it fails with a validation error they don't understand. They either find someone who knows about the field, or they find a workaround. The validation block is real. The problem is that the field should be visible and required from the moment the work order is classified as major repair — not revealed as a surprise when RTS fails at the end of the job."

She is not angry. She is precise. This is exactly the kind of gap she came to find.

> "This is fixable. I'm here because this is fixable. The architecture is right. The interface has to catch up to it."

## What She Will Be Watching During the Embed

Renata has a short list. She adds to it when she encounters something she didn't anticipate, but the core items were fixed before she arrived.

**1. The Form 337 field — when and where it appears in the UI.** Currently it does not appear in the work order edit view for any user role. Target: present from the moment the repair type is set to `major_repair`, marked required, with a status indicator on the work order card (something like "337 Ref: PENDING / ENTERED / N/A").

**2. Whether the RTS block is communicated before the tech attempts to authorize.** She does not want the first indication of the 337 requirement to be a validation error at the RTS screen. The work order should carry a visible status from the moment it's classified as a major repair — open, incomplete, blocked. The RTS authorization should be confirmation, not discovery.

**3. The error message quality on RTS validation failure.** When the block fires because `form_337_ref` is null, what does the tech see? A generic validation error is insufficient. The message needs to name the specific field, explain why it's required (major repair classification), and tell the tech where to go to populate it. She will test this deliberately.

**4. The DER reference pathway.** Athelon's schema currently has `form_337_ref`. She wants to know whether there is also a path to record the DER's information — DER name, FAA authorization number, repair design document reference — for engineered major repairs that required a DER disposition. If it's all collapsed into the same freeform field, she'll work with it but she'll document why structured subfields would be better.

**5. The SRM reference structure.** When a tech records the SRM reference for a structural repair, is it freeform text or does it have structure — manual title, revision level, chapter, section, subject? She expects freeform. She'll push for structure. She knows this is a longer conversation that may not resolve during the embed, but she'll start it.

**6. Tail-number historical view for major repairs.** Can she pull up an aircraft and see all major repair work orders, with their 337 references, sorted by date, without drilling into each work order individually? For a DER reviewing an airframe's structural history before approving a new repair, this view is the starting point. If it doesn't exist, she'll ask for it.

**7. Repair type in reporting.** The DOM at Gulf Coast runs a monthly structural summary. It must segment major repairs from minor repairs because the major repair count feeds into the FAA reporting picture for the Part 145 certificate. If Athelon's reporting layer doesn't filter and group by repair classification, that's a gap for compliance management customers.

**8. The printed maintenance record output.** What the work order looks like when it's exported or printed as a maintenance record is the document that follows the aircraft. It needs to include the repair classification and the 337 reference number. She will generate a test major repair work order, complete it including the 337 reference, and examine the exported record. If the 337 reference is not on the output, it has to be added.

## A Representative Major Repair — What the Workflow Should Look Like

This is the scenario Renata described when asked to walk through what she would want Athelon to do on a typical major structural repair. It is not hypothetical — it is a composite of jobs she has run in the last three years, collapsed into a single sequence to illustrate the documentation chain.

**Scenario:** A King Air B200 arrives with impact damage to the aft fuselage skin — a panel that was struck by ground support equipment. The damage is approximately 4.5 inches in the long axis, with a small crack propagating from the lower edge of the dent toward the aft pressure bulkhead frame. The crack changes the classification: this is not a dent-within-limits situation.

**Work order opened:** She creates the work order. The system should prompt: *Is this a minor or major repair?* Not as a dropdown buried in "additional fields." As a classification step, up front, before task creation. She selects major repair. At that moment, the work order should display a banner or status block: **"Major Repair — Form 337 Required. 337 Reference: PENDING."** The banner should not be dismissible.

**SRM reference recorded:** She locates the applicable King Air B200 SRM section — SRM 51-10-00, Allowable Damage and Repair — and determines the damage exceeds limits. She records the full SRM reference: manual title, revision level, chapter, section, subject, and the specific table that establishes the limits. Athelon needs a structured reference field for this — manual, revision, CSS minimum — not a single text block. She records it.

**DER engaged:** The crack proximity to the pressure bulkhead frame means SRM repair coverage is insufficient — primary structure involvement requires a DER disposition. She contacts Alejandro Reyes. He reviews the photos and dimensional data she sends, issues a repair design drawing number and an approval memo. She gets the DER document number: *AR-2024-B200-0047.* She enters this in the DER reference field on the work order. If Athelon doesn't have a dedicated DER field, she writes it in the work scope description with a header. She prefers a dedicated field.

**337 prepared:** The 337 is prepared before the repair starts. Box 1 (aircraft registration), Box 2 (make/model/serial), Box 3 (certificate number), Box 8 (description of work accomplished — this is where the repair gets fully described: damage found, dimensions, location by station reference, DER-approved repair method, materials and fasteners used, final condition). She assigns Gulf Coast's internal tracking number: *GCS-337-2024-0112.* She enters this in the `form_337_ref` field in Athelon. The work order banner updates: **"Major Repair — Form 337 Reference: GCS-337-2024-0112 (PENDING FAA SUBMISSION)."**

**Repair performed:** Doubler fabricated per DER drawing. Rivet pattern installed per design. Corrosion treatment applied. Panel closed. Final inspection. Everything that matters about the repair — materials, fastener specifications, edge distances, final condition — goes in the maintenance record. Not because the FAA is coming. Because the aircraft is going to be maintained by someone else in five years.

**337 submitted, FAA number returned:** The 337 is submitted electronically to Oklahoma City. The FAA returns a document number. She updates the `form_337_ref` field in Athelon from the internal tracking number to the FAA-issued number. The audit trail — old value, new value, date of update, who updated it — should be retained. The status updates: **"Major Repair — Form 337 Reference: [FAA number] (SUBMITTED)."**

**RTS authorization:** She opens the RTS authorization screen. It displays the full chain: N-number, serial, WO#, work description, repair classification (Major Repair), Form 337 reference and submission status, DER reference, her name and IA certificate number. She reviews it. She signs. The RTS is authorized and the maintenance record is locked.

This is the workflow she is describing when she says she wants Athelon to handle major repair documentation correctly. It is not a wish list. It is the regulatory minimum, expressed as software behavior.

## Questions She Has for the Development Team

Renata came prepared. These are the questions she asked, or indicated she intends to ask, during the embed period. They are documented here because several require product decisions beyond the scope of her individual feedback.

**On field enforcement:**
> "When I enter a preliminary 337 reference number and then update it later with the FAA-issued number, does Athelon retain the history of both values with timestamps? The audit trail on a 337 field update matters if there's ever a question about when the form was actually filed versus when it was entered."

**On the validation error UX:**
> "What does a tech see when they try to authorize RTS on a major repair and the 337 field is empty? I want to see the exact error message text. If it says 'validation error on field form_337_ref,' that tells the tech nothing. If it says 'Return to Service blocked: this work order is classified as a major repair and requires a Form 337 reference number before RTS can be authorized — add it here,' that's a message a tech can act on."

**On role permissions:**
> "Who can edit the Form 337 reference field on a work order after the work order has been closed? At my shop, that field should be editable by the IA who signed the 337 and by the DOM. Nobody else. Is there a permission model for that?"

**On the DER as an external actor:**
> "At some point, the right version of this is that DERs who work regularly with a shop have some kind of read access to the work orders they're approving. Not full access. Just enough to see the damage documentation and confirm their repair design was applied correctly before the 337 is filed. Is that in the product roadmap? I'm not asking for it now. I want to know if anyone has thought about it."

**On search and reporting:**
> "Can I search across all work orders for a specific 337 reference number? If someone calls and says 'I need information on Form 337 number FAA-2024-XXXXX,' can I find the associated work order in Athelon in ten seconds? That should be a basic lookup."

These questions do not all have answers yet. Several of them represent feature gaps or design decisions that have not been made. She knows this. She is asking them now so the answers can be thought through before the interface is finalized, not after.

## Known Requirements She Will Surface in the Embed

- `major_repair` classification must surface `form_337_ref` field in work order edit UI immediately on classification — not deferred to RTS attempt
- Work order card / summary view must show 337 status at a glance: PENDING, ENTERED (with 337 identifier), or N/A (minor repair / classification not applicable)
- RTS authorization block on missing `form_337_ref` must produce a clear, specific error message — not a generic validation failure — naming the field and directing the tech to where it must be populated
- Interim / preliminary 337 references (internal tracking numbers pre-FAA document number assignment) must be accepted — the field should not enforce FAA form number format at entry, but should prompt for update when the final document number is confirmed
- DER reference pathway — DER name, FAA authorization number, repair design document reference — must be present for engineered major repairs, either as structured subfields or as a linked document record
- RTS authorization screen must display full documentation chain before sign is enabled: N-number, serial, WO#, repair description, repair classification (stated explicitly), 337 reference, 337 preparer name
- Tail-number-level major repair history view: all `major_repair` WOs for a given aircraft, sortable by date, with 337 reference visible without drilling into each record
- Repair type (major / minor) must be a distinct, searchable, filterable field — not inferred from work order title or description text
- DOM-level reporting must segment structural work orders by repair classification; Athelon standard report templates must support this segmentation
- Exported / printed maintenance record output must include repair classification and 337 reference number as distinct labeled fields — not embedded in narrative text
- Sign-off summary for IA RTS must include her certificate number, the repair classification, and the 337 reference as a unit — these three items together constitute the regulatory attestation and must appear together in any output format
