# Profile: Teresa Varga — Parts & Inventory Manager
**Role:** Parts / Inventory Manager, Piedmont Air Services (Part 145, GA piston + light turbine)  
**Location:** Hickory, NC  
**Experience:** 19 years in aviation parts and supply chain; 11 years in Part 145 repair stations  
**Interviewed for Athelon:** Phase 5 — Repair Station Staff Discovery  
**Interview Date:** 2026-02-22

---

## Background

Teresa Varga came up through avionics distribution before moving to repair station parts operations in 2015. She spent four years at a regional airline's line maintenance department — long enough to learn what a real receiving department looks like — before taking the Parts Manager role at Piedmont Air Services, a 12-mechanic Part 145 shop in Hickory, North Carolina. She has been there eight years.

Piedmont operates on Corridor. Teresa has a color-coded binder system on top of her desk that predates Corridor and still, by her admission, works better than Corridor's inventory module. She has been asking the owner to replace the software for three years. She accepted this interview because someone finally called who was asking the right questions.

---

## Scope of Responsibility

- All parts inbound: receiving, inspection, documentation verification, shelf-life tracking
- All parts outbound: issuing against work orders, coordinating with mechanics
- Vendor relationships: primary contact for 14 active suppliers
- Quarantine area management: holds, red-tag parts, SUP candidate parts
- Serviceable parts: green-tagging, overhaul coordination, core returns
- Shelf-life calendar: manually maintained in Excel, updated every Monday morning
- Life-limited parts log: maintained in a separate Excel workbook, updated per transaction
- Month-end inventory reporting: manual count + Corridor export, reconciled in Excel

---

## Professional Reputation

Teresa is known at Piedmont for two things: nothing gets past receiving, and she remembers everything. Every mechanic at the shop knows that if they try to pull a part without the paperwork being right, she will find out. She's the reason Piedmont's parts traceability holds up every time the FAA comes through.

She caught a suspect 8130-3 in 2021 — a landing gear actuator that came in from an online marketplace vendor with a tag that had the wrong repair station certificate number in Block 17. She pulled the part, quarantined it, researched the cert number, confirmed the listed repair station had been inactive since 2018, and had the part red-tagged and flagged as a possible unapproved part before the work order it was intended for had even opened. The incident resulted in an FAA SUP report. She considers this one of the better days in her career.

## Technology Profile

- **Corridor:** Uses it daily; has adapted to its limitations; considers it "adequate for everything that doesn't matter and inadequate for everything that does"
- **Excel:** Her primary tool for shelf-life tracking, LLP tracking, and receiving logs
- **Email/phone:** How she coordinates with vendors on discrepancies and expedite requests
- **Handheld scanner:** Uses it for bin location tracking; Corridor does not integrate with it, so she manually enters locations
- **Paper receiving log:** Backup system that is, in her words, "never wrong, unlike the software"

---

## Core Pain Points (Pre-Interview Summary)

1. **Receiving inspection is bypassable in Corridor.** A part can be issued against a work order before receiving inspection is complete. She has caught this. Multiple times.
2. **Shelf-life tracking is entirely manual.** Corridor has a shelf-life field but doesn't alert her. She built her own Excel alert system. She considers this unacceptable in 2026.
3. **LLP life accumulation is not tracked across install/remove cycles.** Each work order in Corridor treats an LLP as if it just arrived. Prior accumulated life has to be manually entered, and there's no validation that it's correct.
4. **The quarantine area has no software representation.** Parts in physical quarantine are tracked on a clipboard. There's nothing in Corridor that prevents a quarantined part from being issued.
5. **Suspect 8130-3 flagging is manual and unenforceable.** She writes "SUSPECT — DO NOT USE" in Sharpie on the bag and hopes no one ignores it.

---

## What She Wants from Software

Direct quotes from pre-interview notes she sent ahead of the session:

- *"If a part doesn't have a clean 8130-3 or a completed receiving inspection on file, it should be physically impossible to issue it. Not a warning. Impossible."*
- *"I want to walk in on Monday morning and see a list of everything that expires in the next 90 days. I don't want to have to look for it."*
- *"Quarantine should be a real location in the system. Not a status field no one reads. A location."*
- *"Every LLP should show me, right on the screen: how many hours is it certified for, how many hours has it used in its entire life, how many hours are left. Not the hours from this work order. The total. From the 8130-3 forward."*
