## Background

Dale Renfrow was born in Farmington, New Mexico, grew up watching Lear 24s and Navajo Chieftains work the Four Corners oil patch, and decided aviation maintenance was a career before he had any business making that call. He worked summers at a Farmington FBO through high school — marshaling, fueling, cleaning exhaust stains off belly panels — and enrolled in the A&P program at San Juan College the fall after graduation. He got both ratings at 23, spent three years doing line maintenance on piston singles and light twins, and then took a job at a charter operation in Grand Junction where the fleet was King Airs and the margin for error was thinner.

He's been in Grand Junction ever since. Twenty-four years. Mesa Ridge is a small shop — twelve mechanics, two full-time IA holders, a DOM who has been there longer than the building's current HVAC system. They do annuals on everything from student-trainer 172s to the occasional SF50 that the resort crowd brings in from Aspen and Telluride. King Airs are the bread and butter. Dale has done enough C90 annuals that he can tell you, without looking, exactly which access panels will have fasteners that require a #3 Phillips instead of the #2 the maintenance manual insists on. He's wrong about very little on a C90.

He applied for his IA in 2011 after his DOM retired and the shop was left without sign-off authority for three weeks while FAASTeam paperwork moved through Denver FSDO. He passed the oral on first attempt, took the written two days later, and has held the authorization continuously since. He's had one near-renewal scare — a family medical event in 2019 put him out of the shop for six weeks, and he came back to find he'd missed the 90-day active inspection requirement by twelve days. He submitted a letter of explanation to FSDO, provided documentation, and it was resolved without action. He does not talk about that period lightly, and he does not miss deadlines now.

The IA means something different to him than it does to people who see it as a credential. It is personal exposure. Every return-to-service he signs has his name on it in a federal record. If something goes wrong on an aircraft he released, the first thing investigators pull is the maintenance record, and the first name on that record is his. He is aware of this every time he picks up a pen. He will be aware of it every time he places a digital signature.

He agreed to the Athelon embed because the DOM asked him to and because, when he looked at their workflow mockups, he had specific objections he wanted to be on record as having raised. He said it exactly that way: "on record." He means it.

He got his private certificate in 2008, after fifteen years of telling himself he'd get around to it. He says the certificate made him a better IA because he now understands what it feels like to be the person at the other end of the maintenance record — the person who is about to climb into the aircraft that Dale signed off and fly it somewhere. He doesn't think every IA should be a pilot. He thinks every IA should have thought about that feeling long and hard. He has. He thinks about it every time he signs a return-to-service.

## The IA Renewal Cycle and What It Means to Him

Dale renews his IA biennially through Denver FSDO. The renewal requires passing a written test, demonstrating that he's performed annual inspections in the preceding 12 months, and appearing in person at the district office. He tracks his renewal date the way some people track mortgage payments — on a wall calendar in the shop, in his phone, and in a small notebook he keeps in his tool chest. He has never been late. After the 2019 close call, he also tracks the 90-day active inspection requirement on a rolling basis. If he's three months from a slow period and getting close to the edge, he looks for annuals to take on specifically to maintain currency. This is not paranoia. This is the job.

The biennial written test covers regulations, inspection procedures, airworthiness standards, and maintenance record requirements. Dale studies for it even though he knows the material. His reasoning: "If I'm not willing to study the regulations before signing my name under them, why am I signing my name under them?" He scored 96 on his most recent renewal. He mentions this not to brag but to establish context — he is not guessing at what the regulations require. He knows.

The renewal cycle also makes him sensitive to any system that might create uncertainty about whether his IA is current at the time of a sign-off. He wants Athelon's system to verify IA currency before allowing the sign-off workflow to open. Not as a courtesy check. As a hard gate. If his IA number is expired in the system, the workflow should stop and tell him, not let him complete a sign-off that will later be questioned.

## Aircraft He Regularly Inspects

Dale's annual inspection volume runs roughly 40–55 aircraft per year, which puts him near the high end for a shop Mesa Ridge's size. The distribution matters:

**Cessna singles (172, 182, T210):** His volume baseline. He can perform a 172 annual in his sleep. He does not perform them in his sleep. This is a distinction he makes explicitly: familiarity is not permission to stop looking. He has found corrosion in a 172 wing spar that the previous two annual inspectors missed. The aircraft had 2,200 hours on it. Nobody was looking at that panel.

**Beechcraft Bonanza/Baron:** The Bonanza is his favorite aircraft to inspect and the one he trusts least. The V-tail variants especially. He has strong opinions about the corrosion-prone area behind the horizontal stabilizer carry-through, the fuel selector valve that mechanics check wrong half the time, and the gear actuator that will fail in a specific and predictable way if the previous inspector didn't torque the jam nut correctly. He finds something on almost every Bonanza he opens. This does not comfort him.

**King Air C90/B200:** Mesa Ridge's primary commercial business. Dale has done enough of these that he has a personal checklist that supplements the Beechcraft maintenance manual, with annotations based on things he's found that the factory didn't anticipate. He keeps it in a binder. He has not digitized it. He knows what that says about him. He also knows the King Air is the aircraft most likely to produce a maintenance record that gets scrutinized by an FSDO, because Part 135 operators fly King Airs for hire, and their records get pulled on a different schedule than private owner aircraft. His King Air record entries are longer and more specific than any other aircraft type in his annual volume. This is intentional.

**Cirrus SR22 / SF50 Vision Jet:** The SF50 is the aircraft he is most cautious about. It's a jet. ## Paper vs. Software History

Dale's paper history is uncomplicated: he was trained on paper, worked on paper for seventeen years, and considers paper the devil he knows. His shop adopted an MRO system called AviWatch in 2018. The implementation was handled by a consultant who left before the configuration was finished. The result was a system that required paper backup on every annual because three of the inspection form fields didn't map to what the FAA actually required on a maintenance record. They used AviWatch and paper in parallel for two years. When the consultant's work finally got corrected and re-configured, it took Dale three days to audit two years of dual records to confirm they were consistent. He found four discrepancies. None were safety-critical. All were documentation errors. He does not consider documentation errors trivial.

AviWatch has been stable since the fix. Dale now uses it for scheduling, parts tracking, and work order management. He does not use it for IA sign-offs. When it's time to sign an annual, he prints the inspection checklist summary, reviews it, signs with a pen, and the DOM scans it. That workflow is, by his own admission, absurd. He knows it. He wants something better. He does not yet trust that what he's been shown is better.

He has also seen the industry shift toward fully digital annuals from the outside — colleagues at larger Part 145 shops who've adopted integrated MRO platforms and moved all IA sign-offs into the software. Some of them like it. None of them have given him a clean answer when he's asked: "What happens if the system generates an incorrect record entry and you didn't catch it before sign-off?" The most common answer is "that hasn't happened." He considers that a non-answer. He will ask the same question to Athelon.

He has seen two other MRO products during vendor demos over the years. Both were excluded from serious consideration — one because it had no mechanism to capture the specific regulatory citation required on a return-to-service entry, and one because the IA sign-off confirmation screen did not display the aircraft N-number at the moment of signature. He rejected that second one in the demo. The sales rep thought he was being difficult. He was not being difficult. He was reading 14 CFR Part 43.

## What a Legally Defensible Digital IA Sign-Off Requires

Dale has a list. It is not hypothetical. He drafted it the week before the Athelon embed began, typed it in his shop's break room on a Thursday evening, and printed two copies — one for the meeting and one for his own records. He calls it his "43.9 checklist" because that's the regulation it maps to.

14 CFR §43.9 specifies what a maintenance record entry must contain: description of work performed, date of completion, name and certificate number of the person who performed the work, the person's signature, and (for IA sign-offs) the IA number. Dale considers each of these non-negotiable fields in any digital system. He will add several more.

**His requirements, as he will state them in the interview:**

1. **The sign-off screen must display, before confirmation:** aircraft N-number, aircraft make/model/serial, the specific inspection item being certified, the regulatory basis for the sign-off (annual under §91.409, or return-to-service under §43.7, stated explicitly), the date, his name, his A&P certificate number, and his IA number. Not after. Before. He needs to be reading what he is certifying, not what was certified.

2. **The signature action must be atomic and immediately confirmed.** When he completes the authentication step, the system must display the completed record within two seconds. Not "submitted." Not "processing." The record, with his information on it, the way it will appear to an FAA inspector. If there is a spinner, he will treat the sign-off as incomplete until he sees the record.

3. **The system must generate a record entry that satisfies §43.9 in its stored format**, not just in the UI. He wants to see the actual stored record — not a prettified view — at least once during evaluation. He wants to know what an FSDO would see if they pulled the record through a data export. He has asked this at every vendor demo. Nobody has ever shown him without significant follow-up.

4. **The completed IA entry must be immutable.** He cannot sign off an annual and have the record be editable afterward without a documented amendment trail. If someone changes a field after his signature, there must be an audit record, a re-signing requirement, and a notification to him. Non-negotiable.

5. **He must be able to export a printed record at any time** that looks like a traditional maintenance log entry — plain language, his identifying information, the work described, the signature indicator. He works with FSDO inspectors who are in their late fifties and will not navigate a software portal during a ramp inspection. The paper has to be available.

## His FSDO History and What It Shaped

Dale has had three formal interactions with FAA inspectors beyond routine renewals. None resulted in enforcement action. All of them shaped him.

The first was a ramp check in 2013 on a Bonanza he had just returned to service. The inspector found the maintenance record entry acceptable but asked Dale to walk through his inspection methodology on the landing gear. The inspector knew more about Bonanza gear than Dale expected, asked questions Dale couldn't answer on the spot about one of the actuator tolerances, and told him to look it up before signing the next one. Dale looked it up. He still has the maintenance manual page, annotated, in the Bonanza section of his reference binder. He is not embarrassed about this. "She was right. I was close, but close isn't the tolerance."

The second was a complaint investigation in 2016. A customer disputed that the maintenance performed during an annual had addressed a previously noted squawk. The dispute came down to the paper record — specifically, whether a finding noted in informal shop shorthand in the previous year's record was the same item the customer was claiming was missed. The record was ambiguous. Dale spent two weeks in close contact with the FSDO coordinating documentation. The investigation was closed with no finding against him, but the lesson was permanent: informal documentation language creates risk. If the record says "checked and serviceable," that means nothing in an investigation. "Inspected per AMM 27-00-00, found within limits, no further action required" means something.

He writes his maintenance record entries in complete sentences. He has written them that way since 2016. He will expect the same of any system that generates record language on his behalf — and he considers auto-generated record text a significant risk unless he can review and edit it before it's committed under his signature.

The third interaction was a 2022 notification from Denver FSDO that they were conducting a survey of IA holders on their familiarity with AC 120-78B, the electronic recordkeeping advisory circular. Dale completed the survey, attached three pages of written comments, and received a form acknowledgment. He does not know if anyone read his comments. He has a copy.

## His Position on Re-Authentication as Proxy for Wet Signature

This is where Dale is careful and specific, and it is the area where his legal thinking is sharpest.

He is not opposed to PIN entry or biometrics as a signature mechanism. He understands the FAA has accepted electronic signatures in maintenance records, and he has read AC 120-78B, which is the advisory circular that covers electronic recordkeeping systems. He knows what the circular requires: that the electronic signature uniquely identifies the individual, that there are controls to prevent unauthorized use, and that the system can authenticate records if questioned. He can cite these requirements from memory, and he will.

His concern is not whether a PIN is *legally sufficient*. His concern is whether a PIN is *personally sufficient for him*.

The wet signature is not valuable because ink is permanent. It is valuable because it is a deliberate physical act that cannot be performed accidentally or distractedly. You have to pick up the pen, find the line, and make the mark. That sequence imposes a pause. That pause is where mistakes get caught. He has caught his own errors in that pause. He has caught other people's errors in that pause. He views that pause as load-bearing.

A PIN can be entered reflexively. A thumbprint can be captured passively. He does not believe biometrics, as typically implemented in commercial software, provide the same forcing function as a physical signature. He wants the system to engineer that pause back into the flow.

**What he wants the re-authentication step to do:**

- Display a full summary of what he is certifying before he enters credentials — not a button that says "Review and Sign" but the actual certification statement, the specific regulatory language, in full, with his name and IA number already populated. He reads it. Then he signs.
- Require a PIN, not biometrics alone. He mistrusts fingerprint on a shop tablet that has been handled by twelve mechanics. He also mistrusts the failure modes — a biometric failure at the wrong moment and suddenly the aircraft is sitting on the ramp with a return-to-service that didn't complete. A PIN is more predictable.
- Make the step impossible to skip or abbreviate. No "remember this device for 30 days." No session token that keeps him authenticated across sign-offs. Every IA signature is a fresh authentication. Every time.
- Log the authentication event separately from the record entry, so that if the signature is ever questioned, there are two independent data points: the record, and the authentication log with timestamp and IP or device identifier.

He has been asked, in a previous vendor demo, whether this level of scrutiny is "a little much" for a software sign-off. He said: "The last thing I signed was a return-to-service on a King Air that was about to carry six people to Denver in January. You tell me."

## What a Successful Athelon Evaluation Looks Like to Him

Dale has a specific benchmark for "good enough to trust." It is not a feeling. It is a test.

Before he will use the Athelon sign-off workflow on a live annual, he wants to run one complete simulated annual end-to-end: open a work order, complete an inspection checklist, trigger the IA sign-off flow, authenticate, view the committed record, export a printed copy, and then have someone else — ideally someone from Athelon's engineering team who has never done the scenario before — open the same record in the system and find every required data field without his guidance. If they can find his IA number, the date, the regulatory citation, the description of work, and his authentication event log within three minutes of first looking at the record, the system passes the readability test.

He also wants to deliberately trigger an edge case: start an IA sign-off, interrupt it mid-flow (kill the network connection, close the browser tab, whatever the system allows), and see what happens. Does the system think the record is complete? Partial? Unknown? He wants the answer to be unambiguous. He has a term for systems that return an ambiguous state after an interrupted workflow: "liability buckets." He will not sign into a liability bucket.

He will not sign a live return-to-service on an Athelon-managed record until he has seen the system survive that interrupted-workflow test cleanly at least twice. He is willing to be the person who runs the test. He is not willing to skip it.

If Athelon passes his evaluation, he will say so explicitly, in writing, to the DOM and to the Athelon project lead. He considers written endorsement from a working IA to be something Athelon should want and is willing to provide it if it's earned. He's said this once, in the intake call, and didn't repeat it. He doesn't need to.

## His Relationship with the A&P Mechanics on the Team

Dale has a specific dynamic with A&Ps who work below him in the sign-off chain, and it is worth understanding before anyone puts him in a room with Troy Weaver or the other mechanics.

He is not a gatekeeper by personality, but he is one by function. When he signs the return-to-service, he is certifying that the work the mechanics performed was done correctly and that the aircraft meets its type certificate. If the mechanic's work was wrong, his signature is still on the record. He has internalized this to the degree that he reviews task cards with a different eye than most A&Ps expect. He is not re-doing the work. He is verifying that the documentation of the work matches what an inspector would need to see — that the correct AMM revision was referenced, that the tolerances cited match the actual findings, that nothing was signed off with "checked OK" when the regulation requires a measured value.

This creates occasional friction. Mechanics who are confident in their work sometimes experience his review process as distrust. He does not experience it as distrust. He experiences it as doing his job. He will say this directly, once, if a mechanic expresses frustration: "I trust you did the work right. This is about whether we can prove it." He does not repeat it.

He will expect the Athelon system to make his review role explicit and workable — that is, he needs a view into a task card that shows him the mechanic's sign-off, the referenced document, the findings entered, and any notes, all in one place, before he initiates his own sign-off. He will not move to IA sign-off from a summary screen that doesn't show him the underlying work. That is not how an annual inspection works, and he will say so.

One thing he does not want: a system where his IA status is visually prominent in the workflow as a status indicator or dashboard element. He has used systems that have an "IA sign-off pending" banner with his name on it, visible to everyone in the shop, counting down. He finds this creates pressure that has no place in the sign-off decision. The readiness of an aircraft for return to service is not a queue item to be cleared. He will make this point in the interview. He expects the design team to hear it.

## Known Requirements He Will Surface in Interview

- Sign-off screen must display full certification statement (with regulatory citation) **before** credential entry — not after, not as a confirmation, before
- Authentication must be per-signature, not per-session; no persistent login carries across an IA sign-off
- PIN required for IA sign-off; biometrics alone are not acceptable at this time
- Completed IA record must be immediately viewable in its stored format, not a UI proxy
- Post-signature record must be immutable; any amendment requires documented trail and re-sign
- Audit log for authentication events must be independent of and linkable to the record entry
- Export to printed maintenance record must be available at any time and must satisfy §43.9 on its face
- System must handle offline / interrupted sign-off gracefully: if authentication completed but record write failed, he needs to know, not find out during an audit
- He wants to see the actual data schema for a completed IA maintenance entry — not a demo, not a screenshot — before he approves the sign-off flow for production use
- The IA number field is mandatory and must appear in the stored record. He has seen two systems that stored the certificate number but not the IA number as a separate field. Both were rejected.
- Before IA sign-off workflow opens, the system must verify IA currency — hard gate, not a soft warning. If his IA is not current in the system, the workflow stops.
- The IA review view must surface: mechanic's task-level sign-off, referenced AMM/document with revision, measured findings where required, and any notes — all before Dale initiates his own sign-off. No sign-off from a summary page that doesn't show underlying work.
- Auto-generated record language must be reviewable and editable by Dale before commitment. He will not certify record text he has not read and approved. Any system that generates and commits record text in a single step is rejected on that basis alone.
- Interrupted workflow (network drop, session timeout, browser close) must resolve to a clearly documented incomplete state with no ambiguity about whether a record was committed. He will test this deliberately before going live.
- He wants a written statement from Athelon describing how the system satisfies AC 120-78B, specifically sections 4 and 5. Not a marketing claim. A technical statement he can attach to his own file.
- The IA sign-off view must not surface as a workflow pressure indicator (no "IA pending" dashboards with his name attached). The return-to-service decision is his call on his timeline, not a queue item.
- He will request a read-only test account with access to a completed sample record before any live use. He will review that sample record against §43.9 manually before signing anything under his IA.
