# Profile: Danny Osei — Work Order Coordinator / Customer Service
**Role:** Work Order Coordinator & Customer Service, Trident Aviation Maintenance (Part 145, GA piston + light turbine)  
**Location:** Manassas, VA  
**Experience:** 12 years in aviation operations; 7 years in repair station customer service and work order management  
**Interviewed for Athelon:** Phase 5 — Repair Station Staff Discovery  
**Interview Date:** 2026-02-22

---

## Background

Danny Osei started as a line service tech at a GA airport in 2014, moved into dispatch and scheduling at a charter operator, and landed at Trident Aviation Maintenance in 2019 when the DOM hired him specifically because they needed someone who could talk to customers without making mechanics angry and talk to mechanics without making customers angry. That's the job. He's been doing it ever since.

He holds no A&P certificate. He has learned enough to understand what a mechanic means when he says "we pulled the prop and the crank seal is weeping" — enough to translate it to a customer without being technically wrong or scaring the customer into pulling the aircraft and taking it to a competitor. That skill is worth a lot to a repair station and is difficult to hire for.

Trident uses Corridor. Danny's relationship with Corridor is adversarial. He uses it because he has to, not because it helps him.

---

## Scope of Responsibility

- Work order creation: takes customer intake, creates work order in Corridor, assigns initial task cards
- Customer communication: status updates, authorization requests, schedule confirmations, billing estimates
- Scheduling: coordinates hangar bay assignments with DOM; tracks when aircraft are arriving and departing
- Parts coordination: bridges customer authorization on owner-supplied parts; communicates parts delays to customers
- AOG response: primary customer contact when a situation escalates to AOG; coordinates with DOM on shop priority
- Discrepancy authorization: calls customers when mechanics find additional squawks during an inspection; tracks verbal authorizations and gets written sign-off
- Work order close-out: reviews work orders before invoicing; catches incomplete task cards or missing sign-offs before billing

---

## Scope of Frustration

Danny answers roughly 20–30 customer calls per day at peak season. Approximately half of those calls are people asking "Is my plane done yet?" or "What are you working on right now?" He cannot answer these questions accurately because:

1. Corridor's work order status is updated manually, and mechanics don't always update it.
2. There is no real-time connection between what's happening on the shop floor and what he can see on his screen.
3. The customer-facing status options in Corridor are "Open," "In Progress," and "Complete" — none of which tell a customer anything useful.

He once told a customer the aircraft was ready, based on what Corridor said, only to find out the return-to-service paperwork hadn't been signed when the customer arrived to pick up the aircraft. The customer waited 90 minutes in the lobby. That customer did not come back.

## Technology Profile

- **Corridor:** Daily use; primarily work order creation, status lookups, and invoice review
- **Email + phone:** Primary customer communication channels; no customer portal exists
- **Excel:** Uses a personal tracking sheet to monitor status of every open work order, because Corridor's list view doesn't give him enough information
- **Whiteboard in the shop:** The "real" scheduling system — DOM maintains it; Danny takes photos of it for his own reference
- **Text messages:** How he actually communicates with mechanics about status, because calling them off the floor is disruptive

---

## Core Pain Points (Pre-Interview Summary)

1. **No real-time status from the shop floor.** He's always a step behind. The customer knows the aircraft was dropped off 3 days ago; he doesn't know whether it's been touched yet.
2. **Customer-facing status is meaningless.** "In Progress" doesn't tell anyone anything. He needs granular, translatable status that he can communicate to a customer without making them panic.
3. **AOG has no dedicated workflow.** When a work order goes AOG, everything should reorganize around it. In Corridor, he has to manually communicate the priority change to every party. There's no system-level flag that tells the shop "this one first, everything else waits."
4. **Verbal authorization tracking is done on sticky notes.** When a customer verbally authorizes additional work, Danny writes it down, puts it in the work order notes field in Corridor, and then has to chase the customer for a follow-up email or signature. There's no workflow for authorization — it's a free-text field and hope.
5. **He can't show customers anything.** Customers ask "can I log in and see the status?" The answer is no. He wishes he could send them a link.

---

## What He Wants from Software

Direct quotes from pre-interview notes:

- *"I want a customer portal so bad. Just a read-only link I can send to an owner. They can see the status, the task list, what's done, what's pending. They stop calling me for status and I can actually do my job."*
- *"AOG should change the color of the work order and put it at the top of every list, everywhere in the shop, until it's done. It shouldn't be a status I have to explain to people."*
- *"When a mechanic signs off a task step, I want to see it. I don't want to refresh. I want my screen to update and I want to be able to tell the customer 'they just finished the engine runup' without it being a lie."*
- *"The customer-facing status and the internal status should be different fields. What the mechanic sees is technical. What the customer sees should be translated — something like 'Inspection in progress' or 'Awaiting parts — estimated 2 days.'"*
