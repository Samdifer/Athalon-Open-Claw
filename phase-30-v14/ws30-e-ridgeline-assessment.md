### §3.2 Compliance Posture Assessment

**Nadia:** "Curtis, tell us about your current compliance tracking. What's working and what isn't?"

**Curtis:** "Okay. What's working: I have a paper card system for ADs. Every aircraft has a card with every applicable AD, the compliance date, the next due date if it's repetitive. I update it manually after every job. It works because I built it myself and I know every entry. What doesn't work: the intervals. When I have a repetitive AD on the TBM — say, the compressor turbine blade ultrasonic inspection, 150-hour interval — I have to calculate the next due date by hand. I have to look at the last compliance entry, add 150, write down the result. If I made a mistake entering the last compliance date, I'm calculating from a wrong number and I don't know it. I caught one of those last year on N88KV — the Caravan. I had a fuel nozzle inspection interval that I'd entered wrong by one decimal place. 1,142.7 hours instead of 1,142.07. The difference was about 40 minutes of flight time but it was still a data entry error in my own system."

**Marcus:** "That's the structural problem with interval calculations in a manual system. The arithmetic is correct, the input data is wrong. Athelon's interval tracking computes from the actual compliance record — whatever the work order says, that's the number the interval runs from. If your compliance record is correct, the interval is correct."

**Curtis:** "That's what I need. The other thing is the Part 145 side. When I work on a customer aircraft under the Part 145 certificate, my work order has to include the maintenance release with my certificate number, the regulation basis, and the authorization for the work. I've been doing that on paper and transcribing it into Excel. It's redundant and I don't like it."

**Marcus:** "Athelon handles the work order documentation fields that correspond to Part 145 record-keeping under §145.219. There's one minor configuration item: the Part 145 certificate number — VRRS3941 — needs to be a pre-populated field on your maintenance release documentation. Devraj is adding that as an org-level configuration field. It's a minor change and it will be in the system before you onboard."

**Curtis:** "That's a reasonable answer. When?"

**Marcus:** "Before Phase 31. Written commitment from me."

### §3.3 Pain Points — Detailed

**Curtis enumerated three primary pain points:**

1. **AD interval calculation errors** — as described above. Manual arithmetic on repetitive intervals; one decimal error on N88KV discovered retrospectively. "I caught it before it was an issue but I shouldn't have to rely on catching it. The system should catch it."

2. **LLP tracking for the PT6A-66D** — Curtis does not currently track PT6A-66D cycle counts in a dedicated system. He relies on the engine logbook and his own memory. "The TBM has a total time of 3,204.7 hours on the airframe, but the engine cycle count is different — it's a faster-cycling aircraft than the Caravan. I can look at the engine log and count the cycles but I don't have a running tally anywhere. If I'm off by 200 cycles on a 30,000-cycle disk, that's my problem."

3. **Part 145 documentation redundancy** — double-entering work order data into paper and Excel. "My mechanics spend 40 minutes a day on paperwork that should take 10 minutes."

**Nadia:** "All three are problems we solve. The interval calculation is F-1.4-A — it's live now. LLP tracking for the PT6A-66D — we'll build the template in Sprint 3 timeline. The Part 145 documentation — that's the certificate number config change plus the work order fields Devraj is adding. None of these are blockers for your onboarding."
