# Athelon Field Dispatch — No. 12

**Date:** 2026-05-08
**Filed from:** Grand Junction CO (KGJT)

## When the Audit Finds Something

There's a version of this story where nothing happens.

In that version, Dale Renfrow opens N416AB's logbooks on the morning of April 28th, works through the airworthiness limitations items one by one, and everything comes back green. The compressor impeller has 11,580 cycles left. The engine mounts have 9,952 hours of remaining life. The rudder pedal torque tube is a bit closer to its limit than ideal but manageable. Dale closes the logbooks, Marcus signs off on the template, Devraj saves the last entry, and everybody goes home knowing the first turbine-type ALS audit in Athelon came in clean.

That's the version compliance software is designed to guarantee: that you looked, and everything was fine.

But that's not what happened on April 28th.

---

I drove up to Grand Junction the night before. Dale Renfrow is not the kind of person who makes small talk, but when I told him I wanted to be in the room for the first turbine ALS audit, he said: "Fine. You can sit in the corner and be quiet." I sat in the corner and was quiet.

The RMTS hangar smells like turbine oil and hydraulic fluid and the faint chemical signature of RTV silicone, which is the smell of every serious aircraft maintenance shop I've ever been in. N416AB was parked in the middle of the floor, cowling open, looking like an aircraft that has been well-maintained — which it has, by Dale, for eight years.

Marcus Webb was at a folding table with his documents. Devraj Anand had a laptop. Dale had the logbooks in a stack the height of a coffee can. They started at 08:00 sharp.

---

I'm going to describe what happened with line item 17, because it's the finding that matters and because the way it was caught is the whole argument.

Item 17 on the N416AB ALS worksheet is the fuel selector valve: part number 9924721-1, life limit 12,000 cycles. A fuel selector valve is the part you move when you need to switch between fuel tanks. On a Caravan, it cycles essentially once per flight — select the required tank before takeoff, verify, maybe adjust during the flight. 12,000 cycles is a generous limit. At N416AB's utilization, it's roughly 4,000 flight days.

When Dale read out the number from the logbook, he said: "Eleven thousand eight hundred forty cycles."

The remaining life on that valve, against a 12,000-cycle limit: 160 cycles.

At 2.8 legs per day, 160 cycles is 57 operational days from the audit. Not weeks. Not months. 57 days.

"There it is," Dale said. He didn't say it dramatically. He said it the way you say something when you already suspected it and are now looking at confirmation.

Here's what made that moment possible: Dale had pre-flagged the valve in an email to Marcus two days earlier. He knew it was close. His spreadsheet had 11,800 cycles — not 11,840 — because the last entry hadn't been updated after three recent legs. The actual number, when he pulled the logbook that morning, was 11,840. Forty cycles closer to the limit than his own tracking system showed.

If the cycle count accumulates at the operational rate and nobody catches the discrepancy, that 40-cycle tracking gap becomes the difference between "managed replacement" and "exceeded limit." Between a scheduled work order in May and a phone call to the FAA in July.

---

What Athelon did on April 28th was simple: it provided the occasion for the conversation. Every ALS item Marcus had scoped was in the audit worksheet. Every item required a number from a logbook. When the number for item 17 came out of the logbook, it was visible — not buried in a row on a spreadsheet that hadn't been opened since the last logbook entry, but in a system where every entry is part of a compliance record that doesn't let you skip lines.

Dale opened WO-RMTS-003 before the audit session was half over. He called Ray Escondido at Crimson Mesa Aviation during a break. He had a part on order by noon.

Marcus put it plainly after the session: "You're right. At 160 cycles, this is a near-term replacement. If you fly this aircraft for six more weeks without addressing it, you will either exceed the limit or be in an engineering analysis situation."

Dale: "Neither is where you want to be."

Correct.

---

I've now sat in on four full-day technical sessions at Athelon customer shops. I've watched data entry sessions at Fort Worth and Scottsdale and Grand Junction. I've watched work order closures and compliance debrief calls. I've watched the system catch an overdue item at Lone Star Rotorcraft that Sandra Okafor's spreadsheet had wrong by 112.8 hours.

The pattern I keep seeing is this: the shops that most need this software are the ones where the DOM is already doing a credible job with spreadsheets and paper cards and sheer professional attention. Dale Renfrow is good. He pre-flagged item 17 — he knew something was off. Sandra Okafor is good. She had every CMR in a spreadsheet, she just had one of the numbers wrong.

The spreadsheets fail not because the people using them are sloppy. They fail because spreadsheets require arithmetic and arithmetic requires correct input data and correct input data requires that you never mis-enter a number, never forget an update, never have a decimal point drift one place left on a day when you were dealing with three other problems simultaneously.

That's not a solvable human problem. It's a structural one.

---

The audit finished at 14:35. All 29 ALS items for N416AB were in the system. Marcus had signed off the template. Devraj had activated the N208MP and N208TZ boards from the same template with aircraft-specific values. Three Caravans, 87 ALS line items, first turbine fleet in Athelon.

Dale shook hands with Marcus at the door of the hangar. He didn't say anything for a moment. Then:

"Same thing you told Jonas when you built the IA re-auth workflow: the mechanic who does the work right without anyone watching — that's who this is for. The system doesn't watch. But it remembers. And it does the math."

He went back inside to close up the shop.

I stood in the parking lot of a general aviation hangar in Grand Junction, Colorado, and thought about a fuel selector valve with 160 cycles left on it, and what it means that someone caught it.
