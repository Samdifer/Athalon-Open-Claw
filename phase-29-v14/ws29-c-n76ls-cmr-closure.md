## §1. Background

### §1.1 How We Got Here

N76LS is a Sikorsky S-76C operated under Part 91 at Lone Star Rotorcraft, Fort Worth TX. Lone Star Rotorcraft onboarded Athelon in Phase 26 (WS26-B) under the DOM Sandra Okafor. Phase 27 (WS27-A and WS27-B) built the compliance tracking layers for the Bell 206B-III and S-76C fleets — the Part 29 ALS audit and mandatory SI tracking that gave Sandra's shop the compliance surface it needed.

In Phase 28 WS28-F, Sandra and Marcus Webb conducted the initial ALS data entry session for N76LS. That session produced 33 ALS compliance line items for the S-76C — Part 29 structural ALS limits, dynamic component retirement times, transmission life limits, rotor system ALS items. Of those 33 items, 31 were COMPLIANT or DUE_SOON. One was NOT_APPLICABLE. And one was OVERDUE.

**CMR-04-70-003:** Fuel Boost Pump — Primary Circuit Functional Check. A 300-hour Certification Maintenance Requirement per the S-76C Instructions for Continued Airworthiness, Chapter 04-70. The item had last been complied with at 3,412.3 hours total time. N76LS's total time at the data entry session: 3,725.1 hours. Hours past due: 112.8.

A compliance advisory was issued by Marcus Webb on 2026-04-11 (the day of the data entry session). WO-LSR-CMR-001 was opened in Athelon immediately. The CMR_OVERDUE DOM alert was active. N76LS was flagged — not grounded by the system, because Athelon does not ground aircraft (the DOM does) — but the flag was clear. Sandra knew.

### §1.2 Why This Matters

CMR-04-70-003 was overdue by 112.8 hours. That number matters not because it is catastrophic — fuel boost pump functional checks exist to detect degradation before failure, and N76LS was operating — but because it reflects the exact class of gap that ALS tracking systems exist to prevent. The check wasn't missed because Sandra's shop is negligent. The check was missed because CMR tracking in helicopter maintenance is genuinely hard. The S-76C ICA lists 47 CMRs across three chapters. Before Athelon entered the compliance data, there was no system that knew when each one was due. There was a logbook, a spreadsheet that wasn't always current, and Sandra Okafor's eleven years of S-76C experience.

Eleven years of experience is not nothing. But experience plus a system that tells you when each CMR is due is better than experience alone.

**This is the first time Athelon's ALS compliance tracking caught an overdue item and then tracked its resolution to completion — from open work order to signed RTS to updated ALS board.** WO-LSR-CMR-001 is the first compliance closure loop in Athelon.

Those three things were separate. The linkage between them was me — my memory, my Friday afternoon review, my knowledge of where each thing was. If I had a bad week, if I was focused on a Part 145 inspection, if a customer called with an AOG — the CMR tracking could slip. Not because I'm careless. Because I'm one person managing multiple systems.
