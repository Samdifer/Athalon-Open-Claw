### §3.2 Compliance Posture Review — Initial Pass

Marcus reviewed Ridgeline's compliance posture based on the pre-assessment data and Curtis's responses to a structured question set.

**AD compliance posture:**
Curtis's paper card system has ADs for all five aircraft. The system is thorough but the interval calculation vulnerability (noted in WS30-E) applies. Marcus asked Curtis to pull the N88KV card and read the most recent repetitive AD compliance entry. Curtis pulled it — AD 2020-18-06 (combustion liner borescope, PT6A-114A), compliance date 2026-03-08, next due at 12,150 hours engine time (12,150 = last compliance 11,650 + 500 hr interval per AD). Marcus verified: the calculation was correct. But the format was handwritten and the engine hours used were from Curtis's memory, not confirmed against the engine logbook during the entry.

**Marcus:** "The entry is correct. I want to note the method for the record: you calculated the next-due by hand and wrote it down without cross-checking the engine logbook in the same moment. That's how errors enter manual systems — not because anyone made a math mistake, but because the input data was from memory. In Athelon, the interval calculates from whatever hours value is in the logged work order, which must match the logbook. There's no 'from memory' step."

**Curtis:** "Understood. And noted."

**ALS posture:**
Curtis has not been formally tracking ALS items for any aircraft. He knows the C208B has life-limited parts (he has the ICA) and has a mental inventory of the high-stakes items, but there is no structured document showing all items, their limits, and their current accumulated values. This is the most significant compliance gap identified at onboarding.

**Marcus:** "You're not alone in this. Most shops at your scale are in the same position. The regulatory obligation is there — the ICA is part of the aircraft's approved maintenance program. But translating it into a working tracking system requires setup time. That's what the ALS data entry session is for. After May 16th, every life-limited item on your turbine fleet will be tracked with a current count and a remaining-life number."

### §4.1 N88KV — Cessna 208B Grand Caravan ALS Entry

**Date:** 2026-05-16
**Method:** Marcus reviewed the validated RMTS N416AB ALS template (29 items, WS30-A); Devraj applied it to N88KV with N88KV-specific logbook values
**Aircraft TT:** 8,491.2 hours at entry
**Engine TT:** 8,491.2 hours aircraft TT; engine S/N PCE-55890 TSOH 3,647.1 hr (last overhaul: 2021-06-14 at Gemini Turbine Services)

**N88KV ALS entry summary:**
- 29 ALS items entered (18 airframe, 11 engine LLP/CMR) per the validated C208B template
- Items at COMPLIANT: 26
- Items at DUE_SOON: 2 (Combustion Liner — 1,352.9 hr remaining to next major inspection limit; Elevator Push-Pull Tube — 9,508.8 hr remaining per hour-based limit)
- Items OVERDUE: 0

**Notable finding during N88KV entry:** The fuel selector valve on N88KV (P/N 9924721-1, same part number as N416AB's just-replaced valve) was entered at 11,114 cycle accumulation, leaving **886 cycles** to the 12,000-cycle limit. This is well within the COMPLIANT range, but Marcus flagged it for Curtis's awareness.

**Marcus:** "Your N88KV fuel selector valve is at 11,114 cycles. The limit is 12,000. You have 886 cycles remaining. At N88KV's typical utilization — say, 3 cycles per day on the charter schedule — that's roughly 9–10 months of remaining life. Not urgent today. But it's the same part we just saw reach 160 cycles remaining on the RMTS Caravan. You now have that number in the system. When it hits DUE_SOON threshold — let's say 300 cycles — the dashboard tells you to order the part."

**Curtis:** "I knew the cycle count was high on that valve. I just didn't know I was at 11,114. I thought I was at 10,800-something. I got the number from my maintenance card and I think the card is behind." He checked the logbook. "Yeah. The card was updated at the last annual in March. The aircraft has flown 314 cycles since then. The card says 10,800, the logbook says 11,114. That gap — I just closed it in real time."

**This was the surprise finding of the day.**

## §7. Curtis Pallant — First Impression

**Curtis Pallant (recorded at the end of the compliance posture review session, 2026-05-22):**

*"The thing that got me was the N88KV fuel selector valve. I knew the number was high. I thought I knew the number. I had 10,800-something written on my card. Turns out I was off by 314 cycles. My card was behind by a full maintenance cycle — I never updated it after the March annual. That's the kind of error that a manual system can't catch because the manual system doesn't know what the logbook says. It only knows what I wrote on the card.*

*"I built that card system myself. I've maintained it every quarter for seven years. I think of it as good. And it had a 314-cycle error in it that I was carrying around, completely unaware, that would have triggered a DUE_SOON alert in a few months if I hadn't caught it here.*

*"I'm not saying the system is magic. You still have to put the right numbers in. But once the right numbers are in, the system doesn't forget, doesn't round, doesn't assume. It knows what the logbook says because the logbook entry updates the counter. That's the structural improvement. After two weeks of using Athelon, the thing I trust most about it is that it doesn't remember things the way I remember things. It remembers them exactly."*
