## §1. Context: From Audit Finding to Work Order

WO-RMTS-003 was opened on 2026-04-28 during the Phase 30 RMTS Caravan ALS Audit (WS30-A). The audit found N416AB's fuel selector valve (P/N 9924721-1) at 11,840 accumulated cycles against a 12,000-cycle life limit — 160 cycles remaining. At N416AB's typical utilization of approximately 2.8 cycles per day on Crimson Mesa's charter schedule, 160 cycles represented roughly 57 operating days from the audit date.

The severity was compounded by a tracking discrepancy: Dale Renfrow's spreadsheet showed 11,800 cycles (the last recorded count), but the logbook confirmed 11,840, and N416AB had flown three additional cycles since the last logbook entry. The actual number at the moment of the audit was estimated at 11,843–11,847. The difference between what Dale knew and what the logbook said was 43–47 cycles — not a lot in absolute terms, but at this end of the life curve, every cycle narrows the margin.

**Marcus's note from the audit (WS30-A §5):** "The difference between 11,800 and 11,847 is 47 cycles — at 2.8 cycles per day, that's 16 days of operation that weren't captured. If the limit is 12,000 and you think you have 200 cycles remaining but you actually have 153, that gap could close faster than you expect."

The audit session opened WO-RMTS-003 before it closed. Dale called Ray Escondido (Crimson Mesa Aviation, Chief Pilot) that afternoon. A maintenance window was established: 2026-05-05 through 2026-05-07. Hector Ruiz — who had previously performed the combustion liner borescope on N416AB (WO-RMTS-002) — was assigned as performing mechanic.

### §3.1 Day 1 — 2026-05-05: Preparation and Disassembly

Hector began with N416AB on jacks, fuel tanks sumped and drained to minimum (trace fuel only, tank caps in place, fuel return lines capped per MM §28-10-00 de-fuel procedure). Cowling removed; fire suppression system safetied per maintenance manual procedure.

**WO-RMTS-003 task card signed off by Hector (preparation steps):**
- N416AB jacked per Cessna 208B MM Jacking and Shoring procedure ✅
- Fuel tanks drained to trace per MM §28-10-00 ✅
- Fuel selector valve P/N 9924721-1 (S/N 2019-0831-11840) identified and tagged — cycle count read from placard: 11,840 cycles (plus 17 cycles flown post-logbook-entry = 11,857 actual) ✅
- Replacement part (P/N 9924721-1, S/N 2026-0120-00001, 8130-3 tag confirmed) staged and inspected ✅

**Fuel selector valve removal (per MM §28-21-00 Removal procedure):**
- Fuel lines disconnected from valve body (both positions — left tank and right tank select lines); lines capped
- Electrical connector for fuel quantity selector circuit disconnected
- Valve mounting screws (4x MS24693-C55) removed; valve body extracted from firewall mount
- Condition of valve exterior: no visible damage, seals showed normal age-appropriate wear

**Retired part disposition:**
The removed fuel selector valve was tagged LIFE LIMIT RETIRED by Dale Renfrow:
> Part No. 9924721-1 / S/N 2019-0831-11840 / RETIRED: Life limit reached (11,857 cycles at retirement; limit 12,000 cycles). Not to be returned to service.

The valve was placed in RMTS's retired LLP storage bin. Dale photographed it and uploaded the photo to WO-RMTS-003 in Athelon (first attached photo in any Athelon part retirement record).

**Dale:** "The fact that we're retiring this at 11,857 — not 12,000, not 12,001 — that's the point. We're retiring it before the limit because we knew the number. If the audit hadn't happened, we'd have kept flying until someone eventually noticed the cycle count was close. Or we'd have exceeded it and had an AD compliance event. Either way is worse than this."
