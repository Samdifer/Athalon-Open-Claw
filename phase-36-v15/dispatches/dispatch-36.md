# Dispatch 36 — The Month After the Win
**By Miles Beaumont**  
**Filed:** 2026-11-25

A milestone always feels definitive on the day it closes.

The paperwork is signed. Someone says “good work.” A card flips from amber to green. In aviation maintenance, those moments matter — because the consequences are real.

But if you stay long enough, you learn the day after matters more.

This month at Athelon wasn’t about a dramatic catch or a last-minute save. It was about what happened after those stories: whether people kept doing the work the same way when no one was watching.

At Walker Field in Grand Junction, Paul Kaminski closed the shop’s first full November 200-hour cycle inside the system. Not as a demo. Not as onboarding theatre. Just as routine. The kind of routine that decides whether software is a tool or a detour.

Across the ramp, Dale Renfrow’s team did what mature shops do: they adopted a revised protocol version, argued over required evidence, and kept the parts moving anyway. In another phase, that kind of argument might have looked like resistance. In this one, it looked like ownership.

At High Plains, Lorena’s stator path moved from “we’re ready when needed” to a real date, real logistics, and a core-return clock nobody is pretending doesn’t exist. The system didn’t remove pressure. It made pressure visible early enough to plan around it.

That is the shift: from hero moments to operating rhythm.

The first chapters of this program were full of proof points — first work order, first turbine lifecycle, first onboarding at a new kind of shop. Those had to happen. They built trust.

Now the harder test has started. Can the network run a boring month well?

In November, mostly yes.

Not perfect. A few notes were thin, one dismissal needed better citation, one mobile display path did something inconvenient and got fixed. Exactly the kind of flaws a healthy system should surface and clear without drama.

If you want one sentence for this phase, it’s this:

**They’re no longer proving the process works. They’re practicing it until it’s normal.**

That doesn’t make for a loud headline. It makes for safer airplanes.

And in this business, quiet reliability is the headline.
