## 1. The Competitive Pricing Landscape

Understanding where competitors price is foundational to positioning Athelon. The market segments cleanly into three tiers.

### Tier 1: Entry-Level / General A&P (Sub-$250/month)

These tools are designed for independent mechanics and very small shops. They are not Part 145-native and lack compliance depth.

|---|---|---|

No startup fees, no contracts. 30-day free trial. Unlimited work orders and inventory across all plans. Differentiator is purely team/location count and storage. |

They win on price but lose on compliance sophistication — no AD tracking, no RTS workflows, no 8130-3 documentation. Shops that outgrow Quantum MX are Athelon's natural upgrade targets.

There are approximately 4,400 FAA-certificated Part 145 repair stations in the US alone; the majority remain on paper, Excel, or aging software with no dedicated compliance layer.

## 2. Pricing Models That Work for Vertical SaaS

### Current State of SaaS Pricing (2025)

- Per-seat pricing is still the most common model (57% of SaaS companies) but declining from 64% in 2024
- Usage-based pricing appears in 43% of models, up 8 points year-over-year
- 61% of companies now use hybrid models (up from 49% in 2024)
- Industry average: 3.2 public tiers plus a custom/enterprise option
- Vertical SaaS commanding 15–30% pricing premiums over horizontal competitors when they handle industry-specific compliance and workflows

### What Works for Aviation MRO Specifically

Aviation MRO software buyers evaluate differently than horizontal software buyers. Three structural constraints shape what pricing models work:

**1. The Unlimited-User Preference**

Quantum MX proved the market with their flat-fee unlimited-user model. Repair stations don't budget on a per-seat basis — they think in terms of "what does this cost per month." A per-seat model forces the shop owner to count heads, recalculate when they hire, and resent every new user add. Unlimited users within a flat tier removes that friction and is a sales advantage in head-to-head comparisons.

However, unlimited users only works if your differentiation is NOT volume. If usage is the primary value driver, consider:

**2. Tiered by Operational Capacity, Not Headcount**

The strongest vertical SaaS pricing ties tiers to business outcomes buyers already measure:
- Number of active work orders / month
- Number of aircraft in fleet or customer fleet
- Number of Part 145 certificate rating categories
- Number of repair station locations

This aligns pricing with value extracted and grows naturally as the shop grows — without the psychological penalty of per-seat.

**3. Module-Based Add-Ons**

Compliance-heavy buyers appreciate a core platform price with add-on modules for premium workflows:
- Core: Work orders, parts, basic customer management
- Compliance add-on: AD tracking, RTS workflows, 8130-3 automation
- Billing add-on: Customer invoicing, estimate-to-invoice
- Analytics add-on: Fleet health dashboards, utilization reporting

This lets you land at a lower entry price and expand ARR as the shop matures its adoption.

### Recommended Model for Athelon

A hybrid approach works best at this stage:

- **Flat monthly fee by tier** (not per-seat) — removes friction, matches market expectations
- **3 public tiers + enterprise** — matches the industry average of 3.2 tiers
- **Annual contract discount** — 15–20% off monthly rate drives commitment and reduces churn
- **Module expansion** — compliance, integrations, and advanced reporting priced as add-ons above the base tier

See Section 7 for specific recommended price points.

## 3. The ROI Frameworks Buyers Actually Use

### The Baseline: What Buyers Need to Justify the Purchase

78% of B2B buyers expect ROI within 6 months of implementing software. For a shop running on Excel or paper, the math is not hard — you just have to help them build it. The CFO (or owner, in smaller shops) will run their own numbers and "not really trust vendor calculations" (58% of CFOs build custom ROI models per Wynter research). Your job is to give them the inputs, not to claim the output.

### ROI Category 1: Labor Time Recovery

This is the most credible ROI bucket because it is concrete and immediately verifiable.

**Paper-to-digital shift in work order management:**
- Digital work orders and approval workflows on tablets: estimated 10% increase in maintenance efficiency per work order cycle
- Work package setup time reduction: up to 60% in documented cases (documented by Oxmaint/CMMS implementations)
- Shift turnover activities alone: savings of up to 10 labor hours per day at larger operations
- Audit preparation: what takes days or weeks (searching paper records, cross-referencing binders) collapses to hours

**How to frame this in a sales conversation:**
> "If your lead tech spends 45 minutes per work order on paperwork that our system automates in 5 minutes, and you run 200 work orders per month, that's 133 labor hours recovered per month. At $35/hour burdened labor cost, that's $4,655/month in recovered capacity — before we count anything else."

**Benchmark to use:** CMMS implementations in industrial settings consistently document 3:1 to 5:1 return on digital investment within 24–36 months, with payback periods of 12–18 months.

### ROI Category 2: Compliance Cost Avoidance

This is often the most emotionally resonant ROI for Part 145 shops because the downside is existential, not just financial.

**FAA civil penalty exposure:**
- Per-violation penalties: $1,100–$75,000 for individuals; up to $1.2M for organizations (per the FAA Reauthorization Act 2024, adjusted annually for inflation)
- A single maintenance record-keeping violation can compound across multiple flights or days
- Certificate suspension or revocation is an existential outcome — it ends the business

**Compliance workflow automation value:**
- Missed AD directives: software cross-references AD updates against the operator's fleet and flags required inspections before the deadline, avoiding groundings and FAA findings
- Continuous audit readiness: digital systems maintain time-stamped, user-attributed, document-linked audit trails — audits become "straightforward verification" instead of weeks-long record hunts
- RTS signoff tracking: eliminates the risk of releasing an aircraft with incomplete sign-offs

**How to frame this:**
> "A single FAA finding for a maintenance record violation is a minimum $10,000 penalty and the distraction cost of an enforcement investigation. One missed AD can ground an aircraft. Our compliance module is insurance you never want to collect on but can't afford not to have."

### ROI Category 3: Revenue Recovery

Less cited but meaningful for shops with billing and scheduling workflow problems.

- Invoice leakage from poor time/parts tracking: shops running on paper routinely under-bill for labor and fail to capture all parts consumed. A study of services businesses found 10–15% of billable work goes un-invoiced due to tracking gaps.
- Turnaround time improvement: 30% reduction in job location/tracking time documented in MRO digital deployments; faster TAT means more throughput capacity
- Customer retention via portal visibility: customers who can see work status and history are measurably more likely to return (no MRO-specific data, but consistent across professional services verticals)

**How to frame this:**
> "If you're running $2M in annual revenue and capturing 10% more of billable labor through accurate time tracking, that's $200K in recovered revenue against a $15K/year software investment."

### ROI Category 4: Management Overhead Reduction

This resonates with shop managers and owners, not technicians.

- One skilled maintenance planner can manage 3–4 high-utilization aircraft with proper software vs. 1–2 without it (ASA/SAM documentation)
- Reporting that previously required hours of manual data assembly becomes real-time dashboard access
- Staff turnover knowledge transfer: all records, procedures, and history live in the system, not in someone's head or file cabinet

### Building a Quick ROI Calculator for Sales Calls

When you're in a conversation with a shop owner or manager, collect these inputs:

| Input | Example |
|---|---|
| Number of technicians | 8 |
| Average burdened labor cost/hour | $38/hour |
| Work orders per month | 120 |
| Average minutes of paperwork per WO | 45 min |
| Annual revenue | $1.8M |
| % of work they believe goes un-invoiced | 8% |
| Hours spent on compliance prep per audit | 40 hours |
| Audits per year | 2 |

Then model:
- **Labor time recovery:** (45–8 min saved) × 120 WOs × $38/60 = $2,812/month
- **Revenue recovery:** $1.8M × 8% × 50% capture rate = $72,000/year
- **Compliance prep:** 40 hrs × 2 audits × $38 = $3,040/year avoided
- **Total:** ~$110K/year in identified value against a $15K/year Athelon subscription = **7:1 ROI**

This is conservative. Put it in a one-page leave-behind and let the buyer adjust the inputs themselves.

## 4. Total Cost of Ownership — The Full Picture

One of the strongest plays Athelon can make is radical TCO transparency. Every competitor either hides costs or buries them in an enterprise RFP process. Being the vendor who shows the full picture upfront builds trust and disqualifies competitors who haven't been honest.

### The Typical MRO Software TCO Breakdown

#### Subscription/License Costs (Year 1–3)

| Cost Type | Range for Small-Medium Shops |
|---|---|
| Annual subscription fee | $5,000–$75,000/year |
| Per-seat overages (if applicable) | $0 if flat-fee; $50–$300/user/month if seat-based |
| Module add-ons | $500–$5,000/year per module |

#### Implementation Costs (One-Time)

Implementation is where the TCO gap between competitors widens dramatically. Legacy MRO vendors charge significantly for setup.

| Cost Type | Competitor Range | Athelon Target |
|---|---|---|
| Initial setup and configuration | $5,000–$50,000 | Self-serve or $0–$2,000 guided |
| Data migration (from paper/Excel/legacy) | $2,000–$15,000 | Included or flat $1,500 |
| Workflow customization | $5,000–$100,000 | Configuration, not code |
| API/integration setup | $1,000–$25,000 | Native QuickBooks/API; free |
| Go-live support | $500–$5,000 | Included in onboarding |

A CORRIDOR or legacy MRO implementation for a 20-person shop can realistically cost $15,000–$40,000 before the first monthly bill. This is the TCO landmine buyers don't see until they're committed. Athelon should make this comparison explicit.

#### Training Costs

| Training Type | Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Per-user training (vendor-delivered) | $500–$1,000/user for enterprise MRO | AMOS-level estimate |
| Time-cost of in-house training | 8–24 hours per user × burdened rate | Hidden cost — rarely calculated |
| Productivity dip during ramp-up | 2–6 weeks at ~20% efficiency reduction | Varies by system complexity |

**Athelon's advantage:** A modern, role-specific UI designed around actual shop workflows dramatically reduces time-to-competency. If a tech can be functional in 2 hours vs. 2 days, the training cost delta alone can exceed the annual subscription difference.

#### Ongoing Admin and Maintenance

| Ongoing Cost | Legacy Systems | Cloud-Native SaaS |
|---|---|---|

| Database backup and recovery | IT responsibility | Vendor responsibility |

Large operators have documented 25–30% reductions in 5-year TCO when moving to managed cloud hosting from on-premises deployments.

#### Integration Costs

Most shops need at minimum:
- Accounting integration (QuickBooks is the standard for small/mid-market)
- Parts suppliers (ordering workflow integration)

Quantum MX includes QuickBooks Online integration across all tiers. Athelon should match or exceed this. Custom integrations for enterprise (ERP systems, inventory management platforms) can be quoted separately.

## 5. Budget Approval: Who Signs Off and When

Understanding the internal approval structure prevents stalled deals and helps you size and structure proposals appropriately.

### Decision Maker Profiles at a Part 145 Repair Station

The typical buying dynamic at a small-to-medium independent repair station looks different from a large enterprise software sale. There are usually 2–4 decision influencers, not the 10–11 found at enterprise companies.

| Stakeholder | Role in Purchase | What They Care About |
|---|---|---|
| **Owner / Director of Maintenance (DOM)** | Economic buyer. Final yes/no. | Revenue impact, liability exposure, "does this solve my actual problem" |
| **Shop Manager / Lead Tech** | Champion or blocker. Often initiates the search. | Workflow fit, not disrupting operations, tech adoption by team |
| **Accountant / Controller** | Financial gatekeeper. Becomes relevant above ~$10K/year. | Subscription cost, cash flow, QuickBooks integration |
| **Quality Manager / QCM** | Compliance expert. Influential for anything touching the RSM. | AD tracking, RTS workflows, audit readiness, regulatory risk |

At smaller shops (under 10 employees), the owner is likely all four of these. Above that, expect the Champion to be the shop manager and the Economic Buyer to be the owner.

### Budget Thresholds and Approval Triggers

Based on B2B SaaS procurement research (Wynter, 2025):

| Annual Contract Value | Typical Approval Level | What This Means for Athelon |
|---|---|---|
| Under $5,000/year | Champion can approve independently in most shops | Target a starter tier here to get in the door |
| $5,000–$15,000/year | Owner/DOM approval, likely informal (no committee) | Still a fast process. One or two conversations. |
| $15,000–$50,000/year | Owner + accountant involved. May require formal proposal. | Expect 30–60 day sales cycle. ROI document required. |
| $50,000+/year | Multiple stakeholders, possible board or investor review | Enterprise motion. Multi-month cycle. |

**Critical insight:** The $5,000–$15,000/year band is Athelon's sweet spot for first customers. It is below the threshold that triggers procurement committees while being high enough to signal serious software investment. Pricing in this range means a shop manager or DOM can say yes in a single conversation without needing to write a formal business case.

48% of companies require six or more approvals for new software purchases — but this is primarily an enterprise/mid-market dynamic. Small repair stations make faster decisions. Your pricing strategy should target the "single conversation close" range for the initial land.

### CFO/Finance Behavior at Smaller Shops

For shops with an accountant or controller involved:
- 66% calculate TCO across 3–5 year periods (ask them to do this comparison with you)
- 76% cite cost as primary factor; 48% cite ROI; only 14% weight implementation feasibility
- 58% build their own ROI models rather than trusting vendor numbers — so give them clean inputs, not vendor-spun outputs
- 36% conduct reference checks with current customers — having 2–3 referenceable customers matters disproportionately early

### What Triggers Procurement Committee Review

Avoid or plan for these triggers when structuring proposals:
- Annual contract value crossing $25,000–$50,000 (varies by company size)
- Multi-year commitments (3+ year contracts draw more scrutiny than 1-year renewals)
- Data hosting of sensitive records (may trigger IT/security review — have your SOC 2 / data residency story ready)
- Integration with financial systems (draws accountant/controller involvement)
- Any "change management" language (signals disruption, raises resistance)

**Structuring to avoid committee triggers:** Offer monthly billing with an annual discount as an alternative. A $1,500/month plan that can be cancelled with 30 days' notice requires no committee — it's an operating expense, not a capital commitment.

## 6. Free Trial vs. Pilot Programs

### The Data Is Clear: Pilots Beat Free Trials for Enterprise

| Approach | Typical Conversion Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Free trial (no credit card) | < 10% for enterprise software | High activation, low commitment, low conversion |
| Free trial (credit card required / opt-out) | 48.8% | Strong for SMB but requires broad inbound volume |
| B2B SaaS industry average (all types) | 18.5% median | Top quartile: 35–45% |
| Structured paid pilot | 40–60% | 78% of enterprise deals involve some form of POC |

Free trials rarely convert to paid in enterprise contexts because they "signal desperation and lack of confidence in the product's value." The structural problem is that a free trial requires no commitment from the buyer, which means they rarely invest the effort to get to value.

### Why Paid Pilots Work

A paid pilot:
- Sets expectations: both parties are evaluating a long-term investment, not kicking tires
- Creates urgency: a 30-day paid engagement gets prioritized; a free trial does not
- Pre-qualifies the buyer: someone who pays for a pilot is 3–4x more likely to convert
- Signals product confidence: you believe in the ROI enough to guarantee it

Pilots with pre-defined success criteria are 3.2x more likely to convert to paid contracts.

### Recommended Athelon Pilot Structure

**For shops under $20K/year potential ACV (SMB motion):**

A traditional free trial with high-touch onboarding works better here because:
- Budget decision is simple enough for a single conversation
- The owner wants to see the product work before committing
- A 30-day fully functional trial with a dedicated onboarding call converts well if you capture time-to-first-value within 48 hours

Structure: **30-day free trial, full feature access, single onboarding session included, no credit card required to start.** Require credit card to continue after day 30.

**For shops with $20K–$75K/year potential ACV (mid-market motion):**

Paid pilot is the right tool. Structure:

- **Duration:** 30 days (not 90 — longer pilots stall; 30 days creates decision urgency)
- **Price:** 15–25% of projected annual contract value, credited toward Year 1 if they convert
  - Example: projected $30K/year contract → $4,500–$7,500 pilot fee, fully credited
- **Success criteria:** Define 3–5 specific, measurable outcomes before the pilot starts (e.g., all active work orders migrated, AD compliance dashboard populated for full fleet, one complete work order closed with digital RTS signoff)
- **Sponsor requirement:** Require a named executive (DOM or owner) to sign the pilot agreement — this surfaces buy-in early
- **What Athelon provides:** Dedicated implementation support, data migration assistance, weekly check-ins
- **Exit clause:** Full refund if success criteria are not met (signals confidence; buyers love this)

**Do not:**
- Offer free pilots to companies in the $20K+ ACV range — it signals lack of confidence and invites evaluation without commitment
- Run pilots longer than 60 days without a conversion decision trigger built in
- Leave success criteria undefined — 62% of failed pilots resulted from misaligned expectations

### Conversion Mechanics After Trial/Pilot

- **Urgency triggers:** Auto-expiration of trial with data export available but account locked until payment
- **Onboarding investment:** The more data a shop has loaded into Athelon, the higher the switching cost and the higher conversion
- **Reference calls:** Offer to connect prospects with existing customers within the first 2 weeks of a trial — peer validation is the highest-converting late-stage tool (72% of buyers rely on dark social/peer networks in their discovery)
- **ROI review:** At the end of a pilot, run a 45-minute "value realization" call with the economic buyer to walk through the ROI model with their actual data populated

## 7. How Athelon Should Price and Position

### Positioning Framework

Athelon occupies a deliberate gap in the market:

> "Enterprise compliance capability at a price a 10-person repair station can afford, with the simplicity to not require an IT department."

This is a distinct and defensible position:
- **vs. Quantum MX:** You have full Part 145 compliance depth (AD tracking, RTS workflows, RSM document management) they don't
- **vs. CORRIDOR / mid-market MRO:** You have no implementation fees, no IT overhead, faster onboarding, and lower total 3-year cost
- **vs. AMOS / enterprise MRO:** You are purpose-built for the US independent repair station market, not adapted from an airline platform

### Recommended Pricing Architecture (First Customers to Scale)

**Phase 1: First 10 Customers (Founder-Led Sales)**

Keep pricing flexible. Your goal is not margin maximization — it's learning which customers get the most value and what workflows they actually need. The right range:

- **Starter:** $499–$799/month (flat, unlimited users) — targets shops under 10 technicians, 1 location, basic Part 145 scope
- **Professional:** $1,200–$1,800/month — targets shops 10–30 technicians, multiple rating categories, AD compliance module included
- **Enterprise:** Custom / $3,000–$6,000+/month — multi-location, integrations, dedicated support

Annual pricing: offer 2 months free (equivalent to ~17% discount) for annual prepayment. This converts monthly MRR to committed ARR, which matters for your fundraising narrative and reduces churn.

**Important note for first customers:** Consider pricing your first 5 customers at a "founding customer" rate (20–30% below standard rates, locked for 2 years) in exchange for a referenceable case study and co-marketing rights. These references will close more deals than any other sales tool.

**Phase 2: Post-Product Market Fit (10–50 Customers)**

Once you have proven value in multiple shops, raise prices and add module structure:

- **Core Platform:** $800–$1,200/month
- **Compliance Module (AD tracking, RTS, 8130-3):** +$300–$500/month
- **Analytics & Reporting:** +$200–$400/month
- **Customer Portal:** +$150–$300/month
- **Accounting Integration (QuickBooks, etc.):** +$100–$200/month

This creates a natural expansion path. Land on core, expand with compliance and analytics as the shop's adoption deepens.

**Phase 3: Scale (50+ Customers)**

Raise list prices 20–30% from Phase 2 rates. Vertical SaaS can command 15–30% premiums over horizontal alternatives. Your compliance-native positioning justifies this. The market data supports $20K–$75K/year for shops your size — you should be in the middle of this range with a clean TCO story.

### Annual vs. Monthly Contracts

- Default to monthly billing; offer annual as an option with a meaningful discount (2 months free = 16.7%)
- Monthly billing removes the procurement committee trigger at many shops
- Annual contracts lock in ARR and improve churn metrics — push for annual once you have a live, happy customer who has passed the 90-day adoption threshold
- Multi-year contracts: avoid offering unless the customer requests it; lock-in at early stage creates relationship tension if the product evolves

### Packaging Principles

1. **Unlimited users on all tiers** — match the market expectation set by Quantum MX; don't make shop owners count seats
2. **Compliance features in the core, not in an add-on** — your Part 145 compliance depth is the reason someone would choose you over Quantum MX; gate it and you lose the primary differentiation
3. **Pricing page transparency** — publish prices on the website; 100% of CTOs and 84% of CFOs visit vendor websites expecting clear answers before engaging with sales; opacity forces people to contact competitors who do show pricing
4. **No implementation fees for the first 2 tiers** — this is a structural competitive advantage; CORRIDOR charges $5K–$25K upfront; Athelon's self-serve onboarding should be zero-friction
