## A. User Sentiment Summary

**Overall Satisfaction:** Mixed-to-moderate. Corridor has a loyal installed base, but the volume of public reviews is strikingly low for a 30-year-old product — suggesting either a customer base that doesn't self-organize online, or one that stays by inertia rather than enthusiasm. Where reviews do exist, the tone is "it works for aviation" rather than "we love it."

**Common Praise:**
- Built specifically for aviation — does not require generic ERP customization to fit aviation workflows.
- Covers the full span of FBO and Part 145 repair station operations in one system (work orders, inventory, compliance, quoting, billing, customer/vendor management).
- Support team is knowledgeable and responsive (particularly praised during onboarding/trial).
- Recognized by the FAA/EASA regulatory environment — inspectors and DAR/DER staff are familiar with its output formats.
- CAMP Connect integration is genuine value-add for shops whose customers use CAMP for aircraft health tracking (which is a large portion of business aviation).

**Common Complaints:**
- Rigid workflows that don't accommodate organizational variation — users must adapt their process to the software, not the reverse.
- Cannot undo completed actions (e.g., closing a purchase order service) — a compliance feature that becomes a usability frustration.
- Feature gating behind subscription tiers — baseline functionality requires upgrades.
- Crystal Reports-based reporting is slow on large datasets.
- Mobile functionality (Corridor Go) is a relatively recent addition — shops that went through the 2010s without it built manual workarounds.
- Steep learning curve despite claims of user-friendliness — likely role-dependent (admins and shop managers face higher complexity than technicians).
- No free trial access to full feature set — evaluation is difficult.
- Slow innovation cadence for a 30-year-old product (44 employees under Hearst).

## B. Top Reasons Shops Leave Corridor (Ranked Pain Points Driving Switching)

1. **Workflow rigidity.** The single most consistently cited complaint is that Corridor forces shops to conform to its process model. Shops with non-standard org structures (e.g., project-team models rather than function-team models) hit this wall hardest. When a software requires organizational change rather than enabling it, shops eventually look elsewhere.

2. **Slow reporting performance.** Crystal Reports dependency causes significant slowdowns on large projects. Shops that grow into higher transaction volumes hit this ceiling and start evaluating alternatives with modern reporting stacks.

3. **Feature gating behind paid tiers.** When core capabilities require subscription upgrades, shops feel nickel-and-dimed. This is particularly acute for SMB repair stations on thin margins.

4. **Mobile / field-tech experience.** Corridor Go (mobile suite) arrived in 2021 — shops that adopted MRO software before then had a decade of desktop-only operation. Competitors (especially Quantum MX, Veryon) have prioritized mobile-first from the ground up.

5. **No true undo / action reversibility.** Once a purchase order service is closed or an action confirmed, there is no rollback. This creates operational frustration when human error occurs.

6. **UI age.** While described as "user-friendly" in some reviews, the interface reflects its Windows-application lineage. For technicians accustomed to modern consumer software, the UX gap is jarring.

7. **Integration friction with non-CAMP ecosystem tools.** Corridor integrates well within the CAMP portfolio (Quantum Control, ILS, CAMP aircraft health). But shops that use QuickBooks, Sage, or other non-Hearst-adjacent accounting/ERP tools report integration challenges.

8. **Pricing opacity.** No public pricing. Custom quotes only. This is a friction point during vendor evaluation — shops can't quickly self-qualify without engaging a sales rep.

## C. Top Reasons Shops Stay with Corridor (Lock-In Factors)

1. **CAMP Connect integration.** If a shop's customers use CAMP Systems for aircraft health tracking (extremely common in business aviation — CAMP serves 20,000+ aircraft), the bidirectional task card flow between Corridor and CAMP is a genuine operational dependency. Switching software means losing this real-time sync, which would require manual reconciliation.

2. **Historical maintenance records trapped in system.** Years of work order history, squawk records, AD compliance logs, and parts traceability data live in Corridor. Migrating this data to a new system is expensive, time-consuming, and carries airworthiness risk if records are improperly transferred. This is the primary migration barrier across all legacy MRO software, not specific to Corridor.

3. **Staff familiarity and hiring pipeline.** Corridor appears in aviation MRO job postings as a required skill. Shops that have trained staff and that recruit people who already know Corridor face re-training costs if they switch. The institutional knowledge is embedded in the workforce.

4. **Regulatory comfort.** Corridor's output formats (work orders, discrepancy records, RTS documentation) are familiar to FAA inspectors and Part 145 PMI relationships. Switching systems introduces regulatory uncertainty — Will the new system's records look right to our PMI? This psychological barrier is disproportionately large given that airworthiness recordkeeping errors have real legal consequences.

5. **CAMP ecosystem cross-sell.** Shops that use ILS for parts sourcing and CAMP for airworthiness tracking are embedded in a Hearst-controlled ecosystem. Corridor is the natural MRO software choice within that ecosystem, and switching Corridor while keeping other CAMP products creates integration gaps.

6. **Inertia at the owner/decision-maker level.** In small Part 145 shops, the decision-maker who chose Corridor 10–15 years ago is often still running the operation. Re-evaluation of core software requires admitting the original decision may have been wrong — a psychological barrier that keeps shops in legacy systems far longer than is economically rational.

## D. Corridor's Target Customer Profile

**Primary target (explicit from marketing and customer lists):**
- Fixed-wing and helicopter service centers / Part 145 repair stations
- Fixed-base operators (FBOs) with maintenance departments
- Business aviation MROs (Gulfstream, Bombardier, Cessna Citation service centers)
- Multi-facility repair station networks (from single-location to enterprise OEM scale)
- Mobile maintenance teams

**Shop size:** Corridor spans "small, single-location service centers to enterprise-level multi-facility repair station networks, to OEMs with thousands of technicians worldwide." In practice, its sweet spot appears to be mid-market shops with 10–200 technicians based on the pricing range ($5K–$15K/year for small teams, custom enterprise above that).

**Aircraft types served:** Fixed-wing and rotorcraft (helicopters explicitly called out). Business aviation (Part 135 operators), general aviation shops, FBOs with maintenance ops.

**Geographic footprint:** Six continents per Continuum's marketing. Strongest in North America given Austin HQ and FAA-regulatory alignment.

**What Corridor is NOT designed for:**
- Commercial airline MRO (too light — AMOS, IFS, Ramco serve that tier)
- Pure parts distributors (Quantum Control is stronger here)
- Startups or price-sensitive shops wanting modern SaaS UX without legacy overhead

## E. Migration Barriers (What Makes It Hard to Leave Corridor)

These barriers apply to leaving any legacy MRO system, but are particularly acute for Corridor given its age and data density.

1. **Maintenance record history migration.** Work orders, discrepancy cards, AD compliance records, RTS sign-offs, parts traceability (serialized part lineage) — all of this data lives in Corridor's database schema. Migrating it to a new system requires: data extraction (may require vendor cooperation or DB access), schema mapping, validation against source documents, and FAA-defensible documentation of the migration itself. Shops rightly fear this process.

2. **Active work order continuity.** Shops cannot pause operations during a migration. The transition window — where new work orders go into the new system while old in-progress work orders remain in Corridor — creates a dual-system period that requires careful management.

3. **Parts traceability chain integrity.** Serialized part history (8130, yellow tag, serviceable tag documentation) must remain unbroken through a migration. Any gap in the chain creates airworthiness questions. This is unique to aviation compared to most software migrations.

4. **Vendor and customer relationship data.** Approved Vendor Lists (AVL), customer aircraft configurations, recurring squawk history, billing terms — all embedded in Corridor and non-trivially reconstructed.

5. **CAMP Connect dependency.** As noted above — shops whose workflow depends on CAMP ↔ Corridor task card sync must either negotiate a CAMP Connect integration with the new vendor, or accept manual reconciliation during transition.

6. **Staff retraining cost.** For a shop with 15 technicians, training all of them on new software during a period of operational pressure is expensive and disruptive.

7. **Vendor migration support quality.** The new vendor's data migration playbook quality matters enormously. Shops that have had a bad migration experience (from a prior system to Corridor, or from paper to Corridor) are psychologically averse to repeating the process.

## F. Competitive Positioning Opportunities for Athelon

Based on the gap analysis between Corridor's known weaknesses and Athelon's architecture:

### 1. Modern, Adaptive Workflows (vs. Corridor's rigidity)
Corridor forces shops to conform to its process model. Athelon can win by designing workflows that are configurable to the shop's org structure — project-team models, function-team models, multi-discipline hybrid models. **Key message:** "The software should work the way your shop works."

### 2. Native Mobile-First for Technicians (vs. Corridor's bolt-on Corridor Go)
Corridor Go was added in 2021 as an afterthought. Athelon's architecture can treat the mobile tech experience as a first-class surface — digital task cards on tablet/phone, offline capability, photo capture on squawk, barcode scan on parts. **Key message:** "Built for the hangar floor, not the front office."

### 3. Transparent, Modern SaaS Pricing (vs. Corridor's opaque custom quotes)
Corridor has no public pricing. Athelon can publish clear per-seat or per-shop pricing that lets operators self-qualify without a sales conversation. This reduces friction for SMB shops that are price-sensitive and time-limited. **Key message:** "Know exactly what you'll pay before you talk to anyone."

### 4. Action Reversibility and Human Error Recovery (vs. Corridor's no-undo design)
Corridor's inability to undo closed purchase order services is a known pain point. Athelon can implement soft-delete, audit-logged correction flows that comply with FAA recordkeeping requirements while allowing human error correction. **Key message:** "Compliant doesn't have to mean brittle."

### 5. Modern Reporting Stack (vs. Corridor's Crystal Reports bottleneck)
Replace Crystal Reports with a live, browser-native analytics layer. Shops shouldn't have to wait for reports to render on large datasets. **Key message:** "Your data, at the speed you need it."

### 6. CAMP-Agnostic Integration Ecosystem (vs. Corridor's CAMP ecosystem lock-in)
Corridor's deepest lock-in comes from CAMP Connect. Athelon can build open API integrations with CAMP (or equivalent aircraft health monitoring systems) so that shops don't have to abandon their existing aircraft tracking setup. By supporting multiple aircraft health platforms (CAMP, Veryon Tracking, MyAirplane, etc.), Athelon removes the ecosystem hostage dynamic. **Key message:** "Bring your existing aircraft health tracking — we play well with all of them."

### 7. Guided, Transparent Migration with Airworthiness Documentation
Athelon's migration offering should explicitly address the FAA-defensibility of record migration. Provide a documented migration protocol that shops can show their PMI. This directly attacks the single biggest psychological barrier to switching. **Key message:** "Your records will be airworthy on the other side. We'll show you exactly how."

### 8. SMB Accessibility (vs. Corridor's complexity floor)
Corridor's complexity ceiling serves large shops but creates a barrier for growing SMBs (5–25 technician shops) that need more than spreadsheets but can't absorb an enterprise MRO implementation. Athelon's onboarding should be completable in days, not months. **Key message:** "Go live in a week, not a year."

## G. Corridor Feature Gaps (What Users Wish Corridor Had)

1. **Configurable approval workflows.** Users explicitly cite the inability to route approvals through custom organizational structures. Corridor's approval flow is fixed to its own process model.

2. **Action reversibility.** The inability to undo closed purchase order services, completed task cards, or other finalized records is a consistent pain point.

3. **Native browser/SaaS access.** Corridor has cloud-based capabilities but its heritage is a Windows desktop application. The SalesEdge Quoting module (launched 2024) was explicitly described as "fully web-based" — implying the core ERP is not. Shops want full browser access for all roles.

4. **Modern reporting performance.** Crystal Reports dependency creates performance bottlenecks on large projects. Users want real-time, fast analytics without long render waits.

5. **Full-featured trial access.** Users cite the inability to properly evaluate the software during trial because advanced features are gated. This hurts Corridor's own sales and signals a product trust issue.

6. **Better mobile experience for technicians.** Corridor Go (2021) improved this but the gap vs. modern mobile-first MRO tools (Quantum MX, Veryon) remains.

7. **Tighter QuickBooks/non-CAMP accounting integration.** Shops not using CAMP-adjacent accounting tools report integration friction. Native QuickBooks Online sync (the SMB accounting standard) would reduce this.

8. **Customer-facing portal.** No evidence of a customer self-service portal in Corridor — customers wanting to track their aircraft status, approve quotes, or sign off on work must communicate through the shop's staff rather than a digital interface.

9. **Real-time scheduling and capacity planning.** No evidence of a visual scheduling engine or technician utilization dashboard in Corridor. Shops manage scheduling externally (whiteboards, spreadsheets, separate tools).

10. **Integrated document management with e-signature for all document types.** While e-signature support is mentioned, the scope appears limited. Full electronic document management (8130-3, yellow tags, customer authorization forms, STCs/AMOCs) integrated into the work order workflow is a gap.
