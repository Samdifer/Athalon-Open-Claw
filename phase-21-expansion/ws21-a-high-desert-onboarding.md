## Pre-Onboarding Context

Bill Reardon came to Athelon the way most people come to anything real: through someone he trusted. Carla Ostrowski called him in January. Not a sales call — Carla doesn't do sales calls. She called because she thought he should know, and because she knew he'd ask the right questions. He did. They talked for forty minutes. He asked about the LLP audit trail. He asked about what happens when the FAA shows up and wants the last three years of records for a specific aircraft. Carla told him to call Nadia.

Bill said: "That sounds expensive."

Rosa said: "It's free. It's also non-negotiable."

## Day 1 LLP Baseline Audit (9:30 AM – 3:15 PM)

Rosa runs LLP baseline audits the same way every time. Start with the aircraft logbooks. Cross-reference against whatever tracking system the shop has been using. Find the gaps. Document them. Build the baseline from primary sources, not from the spreadsheet.

**Aircraft in scope:**
- N3847R — Piper PA-34-200T Seneca II (primary audit focus, twin)
- N6612X — Piper PA-34-220T Seneca III
- N881BB — Beechcraft Baron 58

The first two hours went smoothly. The Baron's records were clean — Dale, the other IA, is meticulous and Bill knows it. The Seneca III had a gap on one prop governor overhaul record that they found and documented. Minor. Within tolerance.

Then they got to N3847R.

**The Seneca II Engine Mount Discrepancy:**

Rosa was working through the PA-34 airframe life-limited components. Engine mount assemblies on the Seneca II have a retirement life under the type certificate data sheet — not a short interval, but not infinite either. She pulled the primary logbook entry for the last mount inspection. She pulled Bill's November Excel file.

The numbers didn't agree.

Bill's spreadsheet showed 4,340 hours on the mounts since last inspection baseline. The logbook, cross-referenced against three years of work orders, showed 4,412 hours.

Seventy-two hours.

Not a lot. Not nothing.

Marco was standing next to Bill when Rosa put both numbers on the table — the logbook calculation and the Excel entry. Bill looked at them for a long moment. He didn't say anything defensive. He said: "Which one is right?"

Rosa said: "The logbook is primary. Always. The logbook says 4,412."

Bill said: "Then 4,412 is what we enter."

Marco said: "Where did 4,340 come from?"

Nobody answered right away. Then Bill said: "The November file. I updated it in October but I was using a run total from the spring backup. Marco's version." He looked at his three printed spreadsheets. "The spring version had an error. I never caught it because I was adding to it, not auditing it."

This is the thing about having three versions of the truth: the errors don't announce themselves. They just sit there, compounding slightly, until someone forces a reconciliation against the primary source.

Rosa entered 4,412 into the system. The LLP dashboard updated in real time. The component now showed correct hours remaining against the life limit. She generated the audit record, tied it to the logbook citation, and locked it.

"That's the baseline," she said. "From here forward, everything builds on that number. There's no version confusion because there's only one version."

Bill was quiet for a moment. Then: "What if it had been six hundred hours instead of seventy-two?"

Rosa said: "That's exactly why we do the audit on Day 1."

**Audit Outcome:**
- N3847R: Engine mount life count corrected to 4,412 hrs. Discrepancy sourced to cascade error in spring Excel backup (Marco's version). Logged and locked.
- N6612X: Prop governor overhaul gap documented. Not life-limited gap — no safety action required; record completeness flag raised.
- N881BB: Clean. No discrepancies.

All three aircraft LLP baselines entered into Athelon by 3:15 PM. Rosa signed the audit record. Bill co-signed. The audit trail is now the system of record.

## Day 1 Afternoon — First Live Work Order (3:30–5:00 PM)

Bill wanted to run a real work order on Day 1. "If we're going to do this, let's do it today," he said.

They chose N6612X — the Seneca III in for a light twin annual. Marco was the lead mechanic. Dale was the IA.

**Work Order WO-HD-001: PA-34-220T Annual Inspection**

Nadia created the work order with Bill. They went through the task card structure — airframe, powerplant (both engines), prop, avionics. The task cards pulled the applicable inspection requirements. Bill added two shop-specific items from their internal inspection checklist.

Marco started working. The first thing he noticed: when he opened a task card on the iPad, the aircraft's current LLP dashboard was visible in a sidebar. He could see the engine hours, the prop hours, the component life counts — all updated from the audit they'd done three hours earlier. He didn't have to open a spreadsheet. He didn't have to ask Bill. The information was just there.

He signed off the first task card at 4:22 PM. The system prompted him for his A&P certificate number — which it already had, from onboarding — and confirmed his authorization level for the task. He tapped SIGN. The signature was recorded with a timestamp, his certificate number, and the specific task reference.

Dale did the IA review at 4:55 PM. The IA re-auth prompt came up — a short confirmation of the specific return-to-service determination, not just a signature box. Dale read it. He signed.

Bill watched the RTS record generate. "That's what it looked like," he said to nobody in particular. Then, to Nadia: "In twenty-two years I've generated thousands of these on paper. That's the first one I didn't have to file."

WO-HD-001 was closed at 5:00 PM. The PDF package was available immediately.

## The Version Moment — Wednesday, 2026-03-04

This happened three days into the week, and it wasn't dramatic. That's what made it real.

Marco was working on a Cherokee that had come in for a 100-hour. He needed to check the engine component history. He started reaching for the folder on the shelf — the one with the Excel printouts — and then stopped himself. He opened Athelon on the iPad instead.

The history was there. All of it, from the Day 1 audit forward, plus the records they'd backfilled from the logbooks during onboarding. He found what he needed in about fifteen seconds.

He stood there for a moment. Then he said, to nobody in particular: "Huh."

Bill was nearby. He said: "What?"

Marco said: "I was going to get the folder. But I don't need to anymore."

Then: "Which version of the spreadsheet was I even looking at before? Like, which one did I have saved?"

Bill said: "The spring one, I think."

Marco said: "The one with the error."

Bill said: "Yeah."

Marco said: "So for how long was I working off wrong numbers?"

Bill said: "At least since October."

Marco thought about that. The seventy-two hours. The engine mounts. How close or far from the life limit they were — he'd have to check, but the point wasn't the margin. The point was that he hadn't known. He'd been working confidently from bad data, and he hadn't known, because the bad data looked exactly like good data when it was in a spreadsheet you'd built yourself.

"There's only one version now," he said.

Not a question. Not really directed at anyone. Just a statement of fact that he needed to say out loud to make it land.

Rosa, who was in the shop that day for a follow-up walkthrough, heard it from across the room. She wrote it down.

## Bill's First-Week Verdict — 2026-03-06

Bill sent Nadia a voice memo on Friday afternoon. She transcribed it.

> "Okay. Week one. Here's what I think.
>
> The LLP dashboard is not optional for a twin shop. I want to be clear about that. I've been doing this twenty-two years and I've managed LLP tracking with logbooks and spreadsheets and I've done it correctly — or I thought I had. The audit found a seventy-two hour error I didn't know existed. That's not a disaster. That's a normal human error. But a normal human error in life-limited component tracking is the kind of thing that gets someone killed if the margin is smaller than you think it is.
>
> The work order flow is good. Marco took to it faster than I expected. Dale had some questions about the IA re-auth prompt — he wanted to make sure he understood what he was actually signing. I think that's the right instinct. We walked through it together and by the end of the week he was comfortable.
>
> The thing Marco said — about there only being one version — I keep thinking about that. We've been running this shop for ten years and we've always had the data. It's just that the data wasn't... coherent. There were three versions of the truth and we were all working off different ones and nobody knew. That's a structural problem that spreadsheets can't solve. Not because spreadsheets are bad, but because a spreadsheet doesn't know what version it is.
>
> Week two: I want to run three more work orders and I want to do the second aircraft's full annual on-system. I'm not going back to the folder."
