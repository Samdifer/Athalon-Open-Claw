# Dispatch #20 — The Second Shop
*by Miles Beaumont*
*Filed: 2026-02-23*

---

Carla called Bill.

That's the whole story, actually. Everything that follows is just what that means.

Carla Ostrowski runs Skyline Aviation in Columbus. She's been on Athelon for about three months. She was at a regional IA renewal seminar in Phoenix — the kind of event where the sessions are fine and the hallway conversations are where the real work happens. She ran into Bill Reardon, who runs High Desert MRO out of Prescott. They'd met before. Same world, different geography.

Carla wasn't there to sell anything. She was talking about a work order — a specific annual on N8432Q, her Cessna 182 — and how she'd checked the parts status from her hotel room the night before and knew, without calling anyone, where everything stood. She used the word *knowing*. She said she'd gotten to a place where she knew, and she'd forgotten how it felt not to know.

Bill asked what she meant.

She said there used to be a state she lived in, running the shop, where she wasn't sure whether her paperwork was right. Not wrong — she wasn't saying it was wrong — just that she couldn't verify it without going back to the file. She'd close a work order and wonder, for just a moment, whether she'd missed something. That wondering was always there. Athelon got rid of it. She opened the app and the answer was there, and it was right, and it had always been right, and she trusted it.

Bill took Nadia's number.

---

Here's what a demo cannot do.

A demo can show you the interface. It can show you the features, the tab layout, the signature flow, the way the RTS button works. It can show you the parts dashboard and the aircraft sidebar and the LLP tracking page. It can answer your questions. It can be thorough, well-prepared, technically accurate.

What it cannot do is tell you how it feels to know.

That's not a criticism of demos. It's just a structural limitation. A demo is a person from a software company showing you software that their software company built. The relationship is what it is. Even if the demo is honest, even if the features are real, there's a gap between "this is what the software does" and "this is what my life is like now." Only a user can cross that gap.

Carla crossed it. She crossed it because she actually runs a shop, she actually closed a real work order, she actually sat in a Phoenix hotel room checking her phone. She wasn't describing a hypothetical. She was describing Tuesday.

Bill heard something he'd never heard in a sales conversation: a peer telling him about a problem she used to have that she doesn't have anymore. He didn't have to wonder whether she was describing his situation. He was living the same situation. The Excel file with three versions — two for singles, one for twins, nobody sure which is current — is the same as Carla's old system, just with different columns. The thing she described wanting, the knowing, is the thing he's been missing without having words for it.

He didn't need a demo. He needed a phone call.

---

I've been thinking about what it means that the product is spreading this way.

Every company hopes for word of mouth. It's in every pitch deck — *"our growth will be organic and user-driven."* But most products don't actually grow that way, or they grow that way at the consumer level where the stakes are low and the recommendation is casual. Telling your friend about a new coffee app is different from telling a fellow DOM about the system you're using to run your repair station. The second conversation has weight. It has professional judgment in it. Carla's not recommending a restaurant. She's telling Bill how she keeps her Part 145 certificate and her airplanes.

That's a different kind of trust transfer.

What Carla gave Bill wasn't information. He could have gotten information from a demo, from the website, from a product sheet. What she gave him was her judgment — earned over twenty-plus years of running a shop, knowing what a good system looks like, knowing what it feels like when the paperwork is right and when it isn't. She lent him that judgment. She said, in effect: *I know what this is supposed to feel like, and this is what it feels like now.*

You can't buy that. You can't manufacture it. You can't schedule it. It either happens or it doesn't, and it only happens if the product is actually good enough that a real person, with real professional judgment, would voluntarily stake their credibility on it in a hallway conversation with a colleague.

The product passed that test.

---

Bill starts onboarding in two weeks. His pain is specific: three versions of a file, no single source of truth, a lead A&P who keeps his own spreadsheet because he doesn't trust the shared one. First thing we do is audit the life-limited parts on his twins and build the baseline from scratch, from verified numbers, not from the Excel file. Rosa Eaton is leading that.

Rosa's framing of it is exactly right: the LLP dashboard isn't a nice-to-have for a shop running light twins. It's the thing that lets Bill know whether his cycle counts are right. For Carla's single-engine shop it was a feature. For Bill's shop it's infrastructure.

That difference matters. As the product grows, it will meet shops where the stakes are higher, the complexity is deeper, the margin for "close enough" is smaller. The product has to grow with them. The LLP dashboard has to be load-bearing for Bill in a way it wasn't for Carla. That's what the second shop is for — not just to grow the business, but to stress-test whether the product holds up under a different kind of weight.

Carla's shop proved the concept. Bill's shop will prove the depth.

---

The moment the product became a business was the moment Carla picked up her phone and called Bill. Not when the first work order was signed. Not when Skyline went live. Not when the first invoice was paid. The work order and the invoice were achievements. The phone call was something else.

It was the moment a real person, with no stake in the outcome, decided the product was worth recommending. That's the only endorsement that matters. Everything before that point was the team building something they believed in. After that call, someone outside the team believed in it too — enough to put her name behind it, in a hallway, to a colleague she'd known for years.

Carla wasn't trying to sell anything. She was just talking about Tuesday.

That's how it starts.
