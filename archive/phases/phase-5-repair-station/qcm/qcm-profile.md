## Background

Renata grew up in El Paso, and the first airplane she ever touched was a battered Cessna 172 at a charter outfit where her uncle worked line. She was fifteen. She didn't go to aviation school immediately — she spent two years at a community college studying general mechanics and picking up Spanish-language aircraft maintenance manuals her uncle brought home, before she transferred to a dedicated A&P program in Tucson. She got her certificate at twenty-three, two years later than most of her cohort, and she has never once considered that a disadvantage. She says she learned to read technical documentation as a skill, not just a habit, because she had to translate it in her head before the English version made the same sense.

Her first eight years were hands-on production: turboprops at a King Air specialty shop in Wichita called Mesa Air Services (no relation to the carrier), then four years at Hawker Beechcraft's in-house service network before they reorganized out of existence. She did structural repairs, progressive inspections, engine changes, prop overhauls. She got her IA in her tenth year because the chief inspector — a man named Dave Krakowski who she describes as "precise and unkind in the most useful possible way" — pushed her to take it. Krakowski told her she argued about records like an inspector, not a mechanic, and that she was wasting everyone's time staying on the floor.

She moved to inspection at thirty-one, under Krakowski at Mesa Air, and found that her instincts about documentation — the thing that had made her slightly annoying to supervisors as a mechanic — were exactly the instincts you needed in QC. She became the QCM at Mesa Air when Krakowski retired in 2017. She's been the QCM at TriState since 2020, when Mesa Air lost a major contract and downsized.

She has worked with the following MRO systems across her career: a legacy CAMP maintenance tracking install used purely for tracking (not workflow), a full-lifecycle deployment of AVMRO Pro (which she calls "almost good"), a home-built FileMaker database at Mesa that was kept alive on life support by one contract IT guy, and Ramco Aviation at TriState. She has opinions about all of them, but the one she returns to unprompted is AVMRO Pro — specifically its discrepancy tracking module, which she considers a near-miss-level failure dressed up in professional packaging.

She agreed to embed with the Athelon team because she reviewed their stated approach to audit trail architecture and thought it sounded like someone had actually talked to a QCM before writing the spec. She is cautiously willing to be proven right.

## Her Relationship with the DOM

This is a thing she has thought about carefully, and she'll tell you about it if you ask directly. If you don't ask, she won't bring it up, but you'll notice the structure of how she talks about her role makes it visible anyway.

At TriState the DOM is a man named Marcus Webb — competent, experienced, primarily focused on production efficiency and customer turnaround. Renata respects him. They do not always agree. The organizational structure is explicit: she reports to the Accountable Manager, not to Marcus, and Marcus cannot direct her inspection and quality functions. This is not a political arrangement. It is a regulatory requirement. Part 145 requires that the quality function have independence from production management, and that independence only means something if it can survive a disagreement.

She has used her independence twice in nine years as a QCM. Once at Mesa Air, where a customer was pushing for a deferral of a hydraulic anomaly that she felt did not meet the criteria for deferral under the MEL. The DOM at the time wanted to find a path that satisfied the customer. She said no. The deferral did not happen. The customer was unhappy. A more thorough inspection the following week found a failed component that would have been a serious problem at altitude.

The second time was at TriState. A mechanic was assigned to an inspection task requiring Citation-specific recurrent training. His training record in Ramco showed the completion. She pulled the actual training certificate from the training provider's portal as part of a random verification she does quarterly. The certificate had a different completion date than the Ramco record, and the discrepancy pushed the recurrency window. She grounded the work order until the record was corrected and re-verified. The mechanic had made an honest data-entry error. Marcus was not pleased about the delay. She documented the incident, the resolution, and the procedural revision that followed.

She does not describe either of these as victories. She describes them as what the quality function is for. She will say, without drama, that a QCM who hasn't needed to use their independent authority is either in an unusually well-run shop or hasn't been looking closely enough.

Her relationship with Athelon's system design is shaped by this: she needs the software to support her authority, not route around it. If the DOM can override a return-to-service gate in the system without her sign-off, the gate is decorative. She will ask about this specifically.

## The QCM Role as She Defines It

Renata does not describe her job as "quality assurance." She describes it as **maintaining the conditions under which the mechanics can sign their names to their work without lying.** The distinction matters to her.

Her authority is independent of the Director of Maintenance, and she has exercised it against the DOM twice in her career — once at Mesa Air (a deferred maintenance item she felt was improperly classified) and once at TriState (a mechanic's training record that did not reflect the actual qualification he was signing off for). In both cases, she prevailed. In both cases, the working relationship survived, though one of them was uncomfortable for about four months. She does not regard those incidents as conflicts. She regards them as the system working correctly.

Her four domains:

**1. The inspection system.** Who is authorized to inspect what, on what aircraft, under what limitations. This is not a spreadsheet she updates at annual review — it is a living document that must track certificate changes, IA lapses, and limitation expansions in something close to real time. If a mechanic's authorization changes and the system doesn't reflect it before the next work order, she considers that a gap.

**2. Return-to-service procedures.** Not just the act of signing the 8130 or the maintenance release — the entire chain of evidence that supports the signature. The task cards, the parts traceability, the inspector sign-offs, the deferred items list, the work order status. She has seen two shops (not hers) have return-to-service signatures challenged by FAA because the supporting documentation was incomplete at the time of signature, even though it was completed afterward. "Afterward doesn't count," she says. "The signature is the moment. The record has to be complete at the moment."

**3. Discrepancy tracking.** Every finding that isn't normal, expected maintenance needs to be captured, described, dispositioned, and closed. Not because the FAA wants it — they do — but because four years from now, when a different mechanic opens that panel and sees something odd, the only thing standing between them and a guessing game is whatever was written down. She has a near-religious view of discrepancy narrative quality. Vague dispositions make her visibly tense.

**4. Training records.** Who is qualified for what, on what aircraft type, with what recency. She has a spreadsheet she maintains in parallel with the official system because she does not trust either system she's ever used to surface expiring qualifications proactively. That parallel spreadsheet is a symptom. She knows it's a symptom. She wants it to not be necessary.

## The AVMRO Pro Incident

She doesn't call it an incident. She calls it "the thing that taught me not to trust module separation."

AVMRO Pro kept its discrepancy tracking and its task card completion records in separate modules with a linkage field — a discrepancy ID that was supposed to tie the finding to the corrective action on the work order. The linkage worked well enough during normal operations. During an unscheduled inspection on an ERJ-145 that came in with a reported pressurization anomaly, a mechanic opened a finding on the aft pressure bulkhead that was related to, but not identical to, the reported squawk. He entered it as a sub-discrepancy under the original work order, which was technically correct per the shop's procedure.

The problem: the print function for the return-to-service documentation package pulled task card completions from one module and discrepancy closeouts from the other, and the linkage logic for sub-discrepancies had a known but undocumented bug. The sub-discrepancy showed as open in the discrepancy module print. The task card showed it as closed. The print package that went with the aircraft showed a discrepancy as open.

The aircraft was returned to service. The customer's director of maintenance noticed the discrepancy three days later. The FAA got involved. No finding was made — the corrective action had genuinely been completed, and the task card signature was legitimate — but the process of demonstrating that took two weeks, a lot of documentation reconstruction, and one extremely tense meeting with a Flight Standards District Office inspector. The software vendor's support ticket log showed the bug had been reported by two other customers eighteen months earlier.

She has never purchased or recommended AVMRO Pro to anyone. She talks about the incident calmly, in the same way a pilot describes a serious near-miss: matter-of-fact on the surface, very precise about details, alert to anyone who seems to be minimizing it.

Her requirement, which she states as a non-negotiable: **discrepancy records and task completion records must be the same record, or must be architecturally impossible to desynchronize.** She doesn't care which. She cares that she never again has to explain to an FAA inspector why two modules show different status for the same finding.

## FAA Surveillance: What She Knows

TriState has been through two full FAA surveillance inspections since she became QCM (2021 and 2024), plus one targeted records review in 2022 that followed a service difficulty report on a King Air. She has also been present for inspections at Mesa Air, including one in 2016 that she considers formative.

She knows the rhythm of a surveillance inspection. The FSDO inspector arrives with a sampling plan — not announced, usually within a general window — and begins with two things: the operations specifications and the repair station manual. They are not looking for perfection. They are looking for consistency between what the manual says you do and what the records show you did. If your manual says training records are reviewed quarterly and your training records were last reviewed nine months ago, the discrepancy is not about training records. It's about whether your quality system is operational.

The 2021 inspection at TriState ran three days. Two inspectors. They pulled seventeen work orders across the prior twelve months, asked to see training records for every mechanic whose name appeared on those work orders, reviewed the repair station manual and the ops specs in full, and walked the hangar floor. They found one observation — not a finding, an observation — about the formatting of the deferred maintenance log, which did not match the format described in the RSM. Renata had updated the log format six months earlier without updating the RSM reference. She corrected the RSM the same afternoon the inspection closed. The 2024 inspection was three days, three inspectors, and zero observations. She regards the gap between those two outcomes as the result of four years of incremental procedure improvements, not luck.

What she watches for in her own records, because she knows the inspector will:

- **Blank or vague fields.** An inspector who opens ten work orders and finds three "corrective action" fields that say "repaired" with no further detail will immediately expand the sample. She has trained her mechanics to narrate, not abbreviate.
- **Timestamps that don't make sense.** A sign-off timestamped before a parts receipt. A return-to-service signed before the final task card was completed. These are usually honest data-entry errors, but they look like fraud until proven otherwise, and the proving takes time she doesn't want to spend.
- **Broken chains of traceability.** Parts without 8130-3s, or 8130-3s that aren't linked to the specific work order. She has a parts-receipt-to-work-order linking procedure that is more elaborate than the Ramco system requires, because Ramco's default parts traceability linkage is — in her words — "adequate for an audit only if the auditor doesn't push."
- **Training records that don't support the signatures on the work orders.** This is the one she watches most carefully. If a mechanic signs off a task that requires type-specific training and the training record shows that qualification lapsed two months ago, the work order is potentially invalid, and she is potentially explaining a recordkeeping failure to an inspector. She reviews qualification status before any work order is opened on a new tail type. Every time. By hand, because neither Ramco nor anything she's used before will do it automatically and reliably.

The 2022 records review was the closest she's come to a finding. An SDR (service difficulty report) on a King Air bleed air duct repair had been filed, but the internal corrective action documentation — the part that shows what the shop did to prevent recurrence — wasn't linked in Ramco. It existed in a separate file the director of maintenance kept. It was complete and accurate. But it wasn't in the system that the inspector was looking at. She spent four hours that afternoon in a conference room demonstrating that the documentation existed, that it had been completed on time, and that the only failure was a filing procedure gap. No finding. But she revised the corrective action filing procedure the next week, and she documented the revision.

## Digital Records vs. Paper Backup

This is where she is careful and specific, and where she diverges from people who think the question is simple.

She is not opposed to digital records. She thinks digital records, when correctly implemented, are superior to paper in almost every meaningful way. They don't burn, don't flood, don't get lost in a file room move, don't become illegible when someone spills hydraulic fluid on them, and can be searched in seconds.

She is opposed to **unverified digital records** — systems where the existence of a record in the database is assumed to be identical to the record being correct, complete, and retrievable. She has seen three failure modes that she describes by name:

**The silent truncation.** A mechanic enters a long narrative in a free-text field. The system accepts it. The database column is defined with a character limit that nobody publicized. The last 400 characters are silently dropped. The print version looks complete. The stored version is not. She discovered this failure mode at a shop she was consulting with, not at a shop she ran. The corrective action narratives on seventeen closed work orders were incomplete. None of the mechanics knew. The system had never shown an error.

**The export failure.** Regulatory records must be producible on demand. At two shops she has consulted with (not TriState), the MRO system's export function — the one that generates the documentation package for a completed work order — had degraded over time as the system was updated. The exports were incomplete in ways that weren't visible during normal operation, only when someone actually tried to use them for an inspection. In one case, part numbers were being dropped from the parts traceability section of the export. The records in the database were correct. The printed document was not. The difference was in a template file that had been overwritten by an update.

**The offline gap.** Work was performed and signed off on a tablet during a period when the shop's WiFi was down. The system said "sync pending." Nobody checked whether the sync completed. Six months later, during a records review, three task card sign-offs from that day are in the local device cache and not in the central database. This is not a hypothetical. She knows the shop. She knows the inspector who found it.

Her position on paper backup is pragmatic: she does not require paper duplicates of everything, which would double the records workload and introduce its own inconsistency risks. She requires that every digital record have a **verified export path** — a function that is tested monthly, that produces a human-readable document that matches the database state, and that output is retained for a rolling ninety days. If the system goes away tomorrow, she wants to be able to hand an inspector a printed document that stands on its own.

She also requires what she calls a **sync closure protocol**: every offline work must have a defined completion step where the mechanic confirms sync status before the task is considered closed. Not the system confirming sync. The mechanic confirming it. Because the mechanic is the one who signed their name, and the mechanic is the one who knows whether the work was done.

## What She Needs Athelon to Do That Current Systems Don't

She has a list. She has had it in various forms for several years. She shared it in the intake interview without being asked.

**1. Unified discrepancy-to-task-card record.** A finding opened during a task card step must be part of that task card's record, not a parallel record in a separate module. Closure of the finding must be a prerequisite for task card completion. The relationship must be architectural, not procedural — she doesn't want to write a shop policy that says "always link your discrepancies." She wants the system to make it impossible not to.

**2. Inspector qualification check at work order assignment, not after.** When a mechanic is assigned to a task that requires a specific authorization — type-specific training, a limited rating, a non-routine inspection authority — the system must check their current qualification before the assignment confirms. Not after. Before. And if the qualification is within sixty days of expiry, she wants a flag that shows up for her, not just for the mechanic.

**3. Return-to-service readiness gate.** The return-to-service function must refuse to activate if there are open discrepancies, unsigned task cards, or parts-receipt gaps against the work order. Not a warning. A hard stop. She has seen too many close calls where RTS was initiated on a work order that showed green in the main view because the mechanic had closed all the primary tasks — but an open discrepancy, filed as a sub-item, hadn't been touched. The main view was green. The aircraft wasn't ready. And critically: the DOM must not be able to override this gate without a QCM co-authorization. If the gate can be bypassed by someone above her in the production hierarchy, the gate is not a gate.

**4. Audit trail that doesn't require her to build it.** When she prepares for a surveillance inspection, she currently spends two to three days pulling records, cross-referencing modules, and assembling documentation packages. She wants the system to maintain that package continuously — a work-order-level audit trail that captures every state change, every signature, every linkage, with timestamps, and that can be printed or exported as a complete, self-contained record at any moment. Not on request. Continuously. The package exists as soon as the work order is opened.

**5. Training record expiry that surfaces automatically and correctly.** Not a static report she pulls weekly. An active alert — in the QCM dashboard, in the mechanic's own view, and in the work assignment flow — when a qualification is within the defined window of expiry. And when a qualification lapses, the system must prevent that mechanic from being assigned tasks requiring that qualification. Passively. Without her having to configure a workaround or keep a parallel spreadsheet.

**6. Internal audit support built into the workflow.** Not a report generator. A way to flag patterns across work orders — the same corrective action narrative appearing repeatedly, the same mechanic having repeated discrepancy opens on a specific task type, completion time anomalies that suggest rubber-stamping. She currently identifies these patterns by reading work orders manually. She wants the system to surface them. She is not asking for AI. She is asking for a QCM-accessible view of work order data that isn't locked behind an admin portal or a vendor support request.

## Current Workarounds at TriState

She keeps a list of things she does manually because the system doesn't do them. She did not volunteer this list in the intake meeting — she mentioned it in passing while explaining her training record concerns, and the interviewer asked to see it. She emailed it that evening. It has eleven items. The relevant ones:

- A weekly Excel spreadsheet of all active mechanics, their current qualifications, the aircraft types those qualifications cover, and the expiry date of each. She updates it manually every Monday morning by cross-referencing Ramco with the training provider portals. This takes about ninety minutes.

- A paper binder she keeps at her desk containing printed, signed copies of the last twelve completed RTS packages for each active aircraft type. She prints these after every return-to-service, signs the print, and files it. She has never needed to use them in an inspection. She keeps them because she does not trust that the Ramco export will work correctly under pressure.

- A monthly test of the Ramco export function, in which she generates a documentation package for a closed work order from three months prior and compares it field-by-field against the paper copy she retained. She has found three discrepancies in four years — none of them serious, but each one something she corrected and documented as a system anomaly.

- A text document she keeps on her personal work laptop with the open questions she intends to ask at each internal audit. She writes them during the week before the audit, based on patterns she's noticed in work order narratives during the prior month. Ramco has no audit preparation function. She has asked the vendor twice. Both times she was told the feature was "on the roadmap."

She does not present this list as evidence that Ramco is bad. She presents it as evidence that no system she's used has been complete enough to remove the need for a QCM who pays close attention. She is hoping Athelon narrows the gap. She is not expecting it to close entirely.

## One Thing She Will Watch For

She will be watching, during the embed, for the moment when someone on the Athelon engineering team — well-intentioned, smart, familiar with aviation at the conceptual level — describes a compliance concern as a UX problem. This is not a condescending prediction. She expects it because she has seen it before. The tendency to resolve regulatory complexity by making an interface feel easier is real, and it produces systems that are pleasant to use and legally precarious.

The specific version she is braced for: someone will suggest that the return-to-service gate should be a warning instead of a hard stop because users find hard stops frustrating. She will have a response ready. She will be polite about it. She will not change her position.

She is also watching for the opposite failure: engineers who are so deferential to compliance requirements that they build a system no mechanic will use correctly under time pressure. She has seen those systems too. A sign-off flow that takes four screens and three confirmations will be worked around. It will be worked around officially — by the shop writing a procedure that preauthorizes batched sign-offs — or it will be worked around unofficially, which is worse. She wants a system that is genuinely easy to use correctly and genuinely hard to use incorrectly. She does not think these are in tension. She thinks most systems just haven't tried.

She will say this in the first working session, unprompted, probably in the first twenty minutes. It is not an opening gambit. It is a statement of what she is here to help with.

## Known Requirements She Will Surface in Interview

- Discrepancy records and task card records must be architecturally unified — same record, no linkage gap possible
- Work order assignment must validate mechanic qualification at assignment time, with QCM flag for expirations within 60 days
- Return-to-service must hard-block on any open discrepancy, unsigned card, or parts-traceability gap — no warning-only path
- RTS gate override must require QCM co-authorization; DOM-only override is not acceptable
- Audit trail package must be continuously maintained and exportable as a self-contained, human-readable document at any time
- Training record expiry must be active and automated, not a report she runs; lapsed qualifications must block task assignment at system level
- Sync closure must be an explicit mechanic-confirmed step after any offline work, with QCM visibility into unresolved sync states
- Export path must be formally tested monthly; she wants Athelon to expose this as a QCM function, not an admin function
- Any field that accepts narrative text must display its character limit and warn — not silently truncate — before submission
- Record timestamps must be generated server-side at the moment of signature submission, not at the moment of local entry
- Internal audit view must be QCM-accessible without vendor support involvement — searchable across work orders by mechanic, task type, narrative content, and discrepancy frequency
- QCM authority must be structurally represented in the system's permission model; it is not a role that should be configurable below the Accountable Manager level
