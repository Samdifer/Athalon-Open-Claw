## Background

Gary Hutchins has been working on airplanes since he was nineteen years old, starting as a line tech at a Piper service center in Wichita, Kansas where he ran safety wire and changed oil until his fingers bled. He earned his A&P in 1998, worked powerplant at a regional turbine shop in Tulsa for five years, and spent three years as a lead tech at a fixed-wing avionics shop before getting his IA and being pulled into a supervisory role at thirty-one. He did not plan to become a DOM. He was handed the job because the previous DOM retired suddenly and the owner needed someone who knew every airplane in the hangar by its squawks. Gary knew them.

He has been DOM at Skyline Turbine Services since 2015. Skyline is a medium-sized Part 145 station specializing in piston singles, light twins, and turboprops. Current complement: 12 technicians (7 A&P, 3 A&P/IA, 2 trainee), 1 QCM (Linda Paredes), 1 parts clerk, and Gary. They run roughly 600–700 work orders per year, predominantly 100-hour and annual inspections on owner-flown singles, with a growing turboprop maintenance contract for a charter operator out of Nashville.

He is called "Hatch" by everyone who has worked with him for more than a week. Origin story: an early career incident involving a cowling latch that he considers too embarrassing to share publicly but which apparently stuck as a name.

## Daily Workflow

### 0630 — Morning Walk
Gary arrives at the hangar before any tech. He walks every airplane in the shop — touches the tail of each one, checks the work area, looks at what's open and what's not. He keeps a mental model of hangar status that he trusts more than any screen. He checks his red-pen status board: what's open, what's due for release today, what's got an AD in play.

He says: *"I already know what the hangar looks like before I open Corridor. The walk is for me. Corridor is for the records."*

### 0715 — Corridor Review
Opens work order dashboard, filters to "Active." Checks for any overnight step sign-offs, reviews whether any task cards have unresolved write-ups from the previous shift. Looks for anything that's been sitting in "in progress" for more than 24 hours without a step sign-off — flag.

### 0800 — Technician Standup (informal)
No formal meeting structure. Gary moves through the hangar, talks to each tech for 90 seconds. What's going on, any squawks, anything you need. He calls it the "friction tour." Finds problems before they become paperwork problems.

### AD Review Cadence
Gary reviews applicable ADs for any aircraft entering the shop before the work order is opened. He pulls the aircraft's AD compliance record from Corridor, cross-references with the FAA AD database for any new issuances in the past 30 days (he checks Mondays), and physically reviews the airframe logbook AD entry page. He does not trust Corridor's AD compliance dashboard alone. Has been burned once.

*"The software shows you what someone entered. It doesn't show you what they forgot to enter."*

### Pre-Release Checklist
Before any aircraft goes back to an owner, Gary walks a physical checklist he laminated himself in 2016:
1. All task cards signed (not just status "complete" in Corridor — he looks at the paper)
2. Maintenance record created and signed by the IA (or A&P for 43.9 work)
3. AD compliance record current — overdue and recurring items reviewed
4. Discrepancy log reviewed — all open items either corrected or documented as deferred with owner notification
5. Cowlings, panels, and access doors physically checked
6. Aircraft total time logged in Corridor and matches logbook entry
7. Owner release form signed

He does not skip this checklist even when he is in a hurry and the owner is waiting on the ramp.

## The Software Incident (Recordkeeping Finding)

In 2019, during a routine Part 145 surveillance inspection, an FAA FSDO inspector reviewed three work orders selected at random. One of them — a 100-hour inspection on a Piper Seneca — had an AD compliance notation that was attached to the wrong work order in Corridor. The AD (AD 2017-04-06, spar strap inspection) had been complied with correctly and was documented correctly in the paper logbook. But in Corridor, the note had been dragged to a different work order during a template copy operation that Gary had done to speed up a recurring customer's paperwork.

The AD itself was complied with. The aircraft was airworthy. But the FAA record in the system was in the wrong work order, which meant the digital traceability chain from the maintenance action to the AD compliance record was broken. The inspector flagged it as a recordkeeping discrepancy under 14 CFR 43.9. It did not result in a civil penalty, but it went into the station's inspection record and required a Letter of Correction.

Gary has not used Corridor's template copy function since. He considers this an indictment of the software design, not just his own error: *"A system that lets you accidentally detach an AD compliance note from its work order and doesn't warn you — that's not a design feature, that's a trap."*

He keeps the Letter of Correction in his desk drawer. Not as punishment. As a reminder.

## What He Wants From Software

1. **Every AD compliance action must link immutably to the work order it was performed in.** Not by reference, not by user entry — by the system. Moving or copying a work order should not be able to silently break that link.
2. **The work order should not be closeable if any AD compliance record is unresolved.** Hard block. Not a warning. A block.
3. **Maintenance records should show on the work order alongside the task cards** — same screen, same scroll, not a separate tab he has to click to.
4. **Aircraft total time should be enforced monotonically** — the system should not let him enter a total time that is less than the last recorded time without an override process.
5. **Morning dashboard should default to "Active" work orders and show nothing else.** He should not have to filter every morning.
6. **He wants to be able to see, at a glance, which work orders have open (unresolved) ADs** without running a separate report.
7. **Mobile access from the hangar floor.** Step sign-offs happen on the floor, not at a desk. A tablet flow would save 30 minutes per day per tech.

---

## What He Fears About Software

- **That it will create a false sense of compliance.** That a tech will see a green checkmark and stop reading the regulation. The software should surface the requirement, not substitute for it.
- **That it will lose records.** His nightmare scenario: a data migration, a software update, a cloud provider failure, and a year's worth of maintenance records are gone or corrupted. He has been backing up Corridor data to a NAS drive manually since 2017.
- **That it will not survive an FAA inspection.** He doesn't care if the software looks good. He cares if an inspector sitting across from him can pull any record in under five minutes without Gary having to explain how the software works.
- **That it will be designed by people who have never been in a hangar.** This is his primary fear. He has been to two aviation software demos and left both times certain the presenter had never personally signed off a maintenance record.
