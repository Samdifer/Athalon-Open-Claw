# Dispatch 19 — The First Work Order

**Author:** Miles Beaumont
**Date:** 2026-02-23
**Title:** The First Work Order
**Deck:** *On a Monday morning in Columbus, a mechanic named Dave signed a step without thinking about the paperwork. For the first time in GA maintenance software history, that was exactly the point.*

---

*By Miles Beaumont*
*Filed from Columbus, Ohio*

---

## I. The Shop, the Aircraft, the Team

Skyline Aviation Services sits on the south end of Ohio State University Airport — Bolton Field to the locals — in a low-profile metal building that has the particular smell of every small GA shop I've ever been in: Jet-A, aluminum dust, floor cleaner, and something underneath it all that might be history or might just be old coffee. There's a parts counter on the left when you walk in. Three bays. A PA-28 Cherokee Archer in the middle bay with its cowling off and a work light clamped to the engine stand.

The aircraft is N4471K, and it belongs to Eastgate Flight Academy, a flight school that uses the airplane to teach basic instrument procedures to students working on their private certificates. It is not a famous aircraft. It is a 1979 Archer with about 2,200 hours on its Lycoming O-360 and a paint job that has seen better years. There is nothing remarkable about it except that it is today's aircraft — and today is February 23rd, 2026, and today is the day Athelon's first live work order is going to be opened, worked, and closed on a real airplane with real sign-offs that will go into a real maintenance record that the FAA can read.

When I arrive at 08:00 local, the morning is crisp and gray. The shop crew is already moving.

**Dave Kowalczyk** — the A&P who will run the inspection — is forty-one years old, fourteen years in aviation maintenance, most of those at Skyline. He is not a verbose man. He drinks bad coffee out of a travel mug that says *Piper Pilot* on the side and says very little until he has something to say. He has done the 100-hour inspection on this airframe model dozens of times. He knows the task card list by heart, which is part of why he agreed to run the first live work order on the new system: not because he was excited about technology, but because he was confident enough in the work to not be distracted by an interface learning curve at the same time.

**Carla Ostrowski** is the DOM and QCM — Director of Maintenance and Quality Control Manager — and has been running Skyline's quality function for eleven years. She built the paper-based documentation process that the shop is now in the process of replacing. She is compact, precise, and somewhat watchful in the way of people who have spent a decade being responsible for what gets certified. She is also, as I will discover later in the day, someone who has strong opinions about maintenance records, which is not a sentence I expected to write when I started this assignment.

**Tina Rourke**, who handles parts receiving and inventory, is at the parts counter when I arrive, which is where she will spend most of her day.

**Ellen Farris** is the IA — Inspection Authorization holder — who will authorize the return to service. She has been doing this for twenty-two years. She arrives at 10:00, when the inspection is already in progress, and parks herself near the tool crib to observe. She is not holding a legal pad. She is carrying only her coffee.

**Marcus Webb**, independent regulatory observer and a name familiar to anyone who has followed the Athelon compliance process, is here as a formal witness. He has his observer log. He will write things down carefully and say very little.

And then there is a second crew who will arrive in the afternoon, when the actual moment of completion is approaching. I will get to them.

## II. The Work Order Opens

At 08:17 local, Dave picks up a tablet from the charging station on the workbench — Skyline's dedicated Athelon device, a newer iPad that Carla bought specifically for the deployment — and navigates to `app.athelon.aero`. He logs in with his real credentials, not the demo account. He has tried the demo account once.

"Different when it's real," he says, to no one in particular.

He navigates to New Work Order. He types N4471K into the aircraft field. The record loads — engine TSMOH, prop overhaul date, last 100-hour date, applicable ADs — all pre-populated by Carla from the aircraft logbooks the evening before. Dave reads the record. He checks the numbers against what he knows from memory.

"Looks right," he says.

He clicks Create.

**WO-2026-SAS-0001** opens on the screen. Status: OPEN.

Fourteen task cards load from the 100-hour inspection template. Dave reads through them with his finger tracing the screen. He knows this list. He has known this list for a decade. He doesn't say anything about it.

He clicks the first card: *Engine — Pre-Inspection Leak Check.*

The cowling is already off the aircraft. Dave goes to work.

---

## III. Signing in Real Time

There is a thing that happens during an inspection — a familiar thing, a thing that anybody who has observed a paper-based maintenance operation will recognize immediately — where the mechanic is working and the paperwork is not. The work is happening at the airplane. The documentation is happening later, at a desk, from memory, at the end of the day when the steps are a blur and the only goal is to get the logbook entries written and get home.

Marcus Webb noted it in his observer log at 10:38 local: *Mechanic is signing steps immediately on completion, not at the end of the inspection. This is correct behavior and contrasts with paper-based practice where signs are often backfilled at the end of the day.*

By card four — the Magneto Timing check — Dave has settled into a rhythm. He does the step, he picks up the tablet, he signs. He does the step, he picks up the tablet, he signs. The tablet is on the rolling cart beside the airplane, and his arm is reaching for it before he's fully finished with the step, the way you reach for a pen that you know is there without looking.

By card seven — *Control Surface Free Travel* — he is not thinking about the tablet.

"When did you stop thinking about it?" I ask him later.

He shrugs. "When I stopped thinking about it."

That is not an evasive answer. It is the only honest one. The moment is invisible from the inside. From the outside, it looks like 10:43 local, when Dave signs Card 7 mid-motion, sets the tablet back on the cart, and immediately crouches to begin Card 8 without any visible transition in his attention. He is doing the inspection. The documentation is simply happening, the way documentation should happen — concurrent, complete, and unremarkable.

---

## IV. The Cracked Gasket

Card 9 is *Exhaust System Inspection.*

Dave removes the upper cowl panel for better access to the exhaust flange on the number three cylinder. He shines his flashlight into the gap and holds it there for a moment. Then he looks at Carla.

"There it is," he says.

A hairline crack in the exhaust gasket on the number three cylinder exhaust flange. Not a catastrophic failure — not yet — but a progressive finding that will eventually become one if it isn't addressed. He has seen it before, on this type in particular. It is a standard finding on aging Archers.

Under the old system: squawk sheet, clipboard, folder, Carla creates a separate record somewhere, someone gets Tina involved to find a part, the documentation trails the work by hours or days, and the connection between finding and disposition exists only in a manila folder that somebody will check before releasing the aircraft.

Dave taps "Open Discrepancy" on Card 9.

He types: *Cracked exhaust gasket, #3 cylinder exhaust flange. Hairline crack observed at 11:02 local. Part number: PA28-exhaust-gasket-3. Unairworthy finding.*

He hits Save.

The work order immediately shows a yellow blocking banner: **"1 open discrepancy. RTS is blocked until all unairworthy findings are dispositioned."**

Dave looks at the banner. He looks at Carla.

"It blocked it. Right now."

"Yes," Carla says.

"Not at the end. Right now."

"That's the idea."

This is — in the context of a Tuesday morning inspection in Columbus with a cracked gasket on a beat-up Archer — a significant moment. The discrepancy exists. The system knows about it. The aircraft is not going back in service until that discrepancy is resolved. This is not a rule that someone will enforce at the end of the day by checking a folder. It is a constraint built into the state of the work order, and it cannot be overridden by forgetting.

Dave sends a notification to Tina: look for the gasket.

Tina finds it in inventory — they have one, new, with a valid FAA Form 8130-3 from Lycoming Parts Distributor. She enters it in the parts module: part number, condition, form type, lot traceability. She uploads the scan of the 8130-3. The file is 7.3 megabytes, which I mention only because there was a moment during the previous day's onboarding session when a 28-megabyte file caused an upload failure and Devraj had to patch the size limit remotely in under four minutes — a small incident that became a useful example of how these things actually work in the field, and which did not recur. The upload succeeds without error.

Carla spot-checks the part record and approves it. Two seconds.

The part is linked to Card 9.

Dave replaces the gasket. Piper PA-28-181 Maintenance Manual, Section 12-3. Torqued to spec. He writes the corrective action in the disposition field. He signs.

The discrepancy closes.

The yellow banner clears.

The close readiness status updates in real time. All fourteen task cards: complete. All sixty-seven steps: signed. Discrepancy: dispositioned. Parts: linked, traceability confirmed. AD compliance: two applicable ADs on file, both current, compliance notes on record.

A green banner: **"This work order is ready to close."**

---

## V. Ellen's Moment

At 13:51 local, Dave calls Ellen over from the tool crib.

"Ellen. Your turn."

She sits down at the tablet. The work order is open. She reviews the task cards — all fourteen of them, each one with its steps, its sign-off timestamps, Dave's name on each one. She reviews the discrepancy record: finding, part received with 8130-3, corrective action documented, disposition signed. She scrolls through the audit trail. Twenty-nine events. Everything Dave did is in there, in sequence, with timestamps, with hashes.

She clicks **"Authorize Return to Service (IA Required)"**.

A modal appears. A certification statement loads — the full text, the specific aircraft, the specific regulation, the implication of the act she is about to perform:

> *"By completing this authentication, you are certifying under 14 CFR §65.95 that the aircraft described in this work order (N4471K, Piper PA-28-181 Cherokee Archer) has been inspected in accordance with the applicable inspection procedures and found to be in an airworthy condition, OR that the discrepancies noted on the maintenance record have been provided to the aircraft owner or lessee..."*

Ellen reads it.

She reads the whole thing.

Then she sits there for approximately fifteen seconds without doing anything. She is not confused. She is not stuck on the interface. She is thinking.

I am in the corner of the shop with my notebook and I write the time: 13:54. Marcus Webb, across the shop, does not write anything for those seconds. He watches.

Ellen enters her password. Full credential re-entry.

The IA sign-off records: her name, her IA number (2019-CE-0441), the timestamp (2026-02-23T18:52:14Z), the certification text, the work order. RTS status: **AUTHORIZED.**

She says: "Okay."

She hands the tablet back to Dave and doesn't say anything else.

I catch up with her by the tool crib ten minutes later.

"Can I ask what you were thinking? Those fifteen seconds?"

She looks at the floor for a moment.

"I was thinking about 1989. There was an accident — a botched 100-hour on a 172 at a shop I trained at, two years before I got my IA. The mechanic signed off the inspection. There was a magneto problem that was documented on a squawk sheet but never dispositioned. The squawk sheet was in a folder. Nobody checked the folder before they released the plane. The pilot was a student. He was twenty-three."

She pauses.

"I've been signing annual releases for twenty-two years. It takes me about four seconds. I think I probably stopped reading what I was signing sometime around 2008."

She nods at the tablet.

"It feels like what signing a maintenance release is supposed to feel like."

## VI. The Afternoon Crew

Rosa Eaton arrives at Skyline at 14:30. She is Athelon's aviation compliance advisor, the person who translated the regulatory intent of 14 CFR §65.95 into the plain language of the certification statement Ellen just read. She has been consulting on this program since Phase 5. She comes in through the side door, looks at the work order status on the tablet, and says nothing for a long moment.

"It closed?" she says.

"Not yet," Carla says. "QCM review still to go."

Rosa nods and stands near the parts counter with her arms crossed, watching.

**Cilla Oduya** arrives twenty minutes later. She is Athelon's Production Integrity Lead — the person who designed and owns the hash verification and audit trail architecture, the person who will issue the production receipt that makes this work order officially the first on the platform. She comes in carrying a laptop and a coffee and her characteristic expression, which is something between focused and serene, the expression of someone who has been waiting for a long time for a specific thing to happen and is not quite ready to believe it's about to.

She pulls up the production monitoring dashboard on her laptop and parks near the door.

"You can sit down," Carla says.

"I know," Cilla says. She doesn't sit down.

Carla performs the QCM review. She goes through the close readiness report methodically. Task cards: complete. Steps signed with identifiable technicians: verified. Discrepancy record: complete disposition, part traceability confirmed. IA authorization: Ellen Farris, IA 2019-CE-0441, authenticated. Audit trail: twenty-nine events, sequential hashes.

She clicks **"DOM/QCM Review Complete."** She is prompted for her own re-authentication. She signs.

**WO-2026-SAS-0001 Status: CLOSED**

The time is 13:59:41 UTC.

---

## VII. The PDF

Carla opens the PDF export.

The document is eleven pages. It begins with the work order header. Then the task cards, each with its steps and sign-off timestamps. Then the discrepancy section. Then the parts log. Then the AD compliance log. Then the audit trail summary.

Then the maintenance release statement on page eleven.

Carla scrolls to it. She reads it. Then she looks up from the screen.

"You know what's different about this?"

She's not asking. She's going to say it.

"Every time I've printed a maintenance record from any system I've ever used, I've had to check it. I go through it against my paperwork to make sure what got printed matches what actually happened. Because sometimes things don't get saved right, or someone forgot to log something, or the print template is wrong. I've been doing that check for twenty years.

"I just read that PDF and I didn't check it once. Because I watched everything go in. I know it's in there. I know the order it's in. I know the hash at the bottom is the hash of what I watched happen."

She scrolls back to the top.

"This is the first time I've trusted a maintenance record the first time I read it."

From the door, Cilla is looking at her laptop screen. She has been looking at it for about ninety seconds. Then she looks up.

"Audit trail events: twenty-nine. Sequential hash integrity: verified. Tamper detection: pass." She pauses. "Record permanence confirmed."

She sits down.

Rosa, from the parts counter: "So it worked."

"It worked," Cilla says.

Nobody says anything else for a moment. The work light is still clamped to the engine stand. The cowling is back on N4471K. The Archer is airworthy.

## VIII. What Worked, What Surprised Them, What They'd Change

Over the course of the afternoon and the following day, I gathered observations from everyone present. These are their honest field notes.

**What worked:**

Dave said the tablet interface was readable from arm's length, which is more than he can say for some systems he's used. He was able to sign steps mid-motion without breaking his physical workflow. The discrepancy card opened quickly and required no navigation away from the card he was on. "I didn't have to remember to write anything down later," he said. "Everything I did was already written down."

Carla said the RTS blocking behavior on open discrepancies was the single most significant thing she observed. "It took that decision out of my hands. I don't have to remember to check the squawk log before we release because the system won't let us release. That's the architecture doing the work that a human doing the architecture wrong was the failure mode for."

Ellen said she hadn't expected the certification statement to affect her. "I thought it was going to be a form," she said. "I thought I was going to read it once and then it would be furniture. But it wasn't furniture. It was still words the second time."

Marcus Webb, whose regulatory observation notes are formal and measured, allowed himself one unqualified line in his report: *"This is a significant result. I have reviewed MRO software systems for twenty-two years. WO-2026-SAS-0001 is the first record produced by a small-shop MRO system that I would present to an FAA Safety Inspector without any preparation or supplemental paper documentation."*

Rosa, who has spent significant time reviewing the regulatory compliance basis of the re-auth flow and the audit trail, said she had expected it to work in theory but had not been certain how it would feel in a real shop with a real mechanic and a real aircraft. "Theory is easier," she said. "You can control the theory. You can't control Dave."

Cilla said the hash verification was clean and the audit trail was exactly what she had expected from the staging tests — which, she noted, was the whole point. "If anything had been different from staging, that would have been news. The fact that it wasn't different is the news."

**What surprised them:**

Dave was surprised by how quickly the discrepancy workflow fell into his existing pattern of work. "I thought it was going to be a disruption," he said. "Finding a squawk is always a bit of a disruption — it's extra work, it changes the day. I thought the system would make that worse. It didn't make it worse. It kind of just... absorbed it."

Carla was surprised that she didn't feel the need to check the PDF. "I've been checking maintenance records for twenty years," she said. "It's reflex. I didn't have to." She paused. "I'm going to have to think about what that means for what I do next."

Ellen was surprised by the audit trail. She had expected the sign-off to be recorded, but she scrolled back through the twenty-nine events during her review and saw every action Dave had taken — time, task, identity — in order, with hashes. "It's like a witness," she said. "Not a document. An actual witness."

**What they'd change:**

Dave asked for native app access on the tablet four times across two days, which I am going to stop describing individually and simply characterize as: Dave wants a native app. The responsive web is workable but he notices the difference. He also mentioned that the aircraft data sidebar could be more visible during task card work — he wanted engine hours and prop time visible without navigating away from the card.

Carla noted that she would have preferred the parts receipt confirmation to be more prominent in the discrepancy record view. She had to scroll to see the linked part, and in a work order with multiple discrepancies she could imagine losing track of which ones had been fully documented.

Ellen noted that the certification statement modal required her to scroll on the tablet to read the full text, which meant the authentication button was below the fold. "I read the whole thing," she said. "But I can imagine someone not reading the whole thing because the button is visible at the bottom and it's easier to just scroll to the button." She paused. "I'm not suggesting making it harder. I'm suggesting making it so the natural thing is to read first."

## IX. On Honesty

I need to say something that isn't strictly part of the story of February 23rd.

I've been embedded with this team since Phase 5, which is to say I've been watching them for roughly two years. I have filed eighteen dispatches before this one. Some of those dispatches were about things the team got wrong.

In Phase 9, a seal-consistency bug in the audit trail let a hash sequence break under specific race conditions. The team caught it themselves during a hardening sprint, not during a customer deployment and not during a regulatory review. They fixed it. But it existed.

In Phases 11 and 12, the team had to substantially expand their regulatory compliance documentation when their initial analysis was judged insufficient by their own compliance advisor. The artifacts they'd written were thin on citation. Marcus's compliance memos — which eventually ran to twenty-eight pages — were the product of slowing down and proving what they thought they already knew.

Why does this matter now?

Because the team that built the re-auth flow, the audit trail, the hash, the pre-close checklist that runs in eleven seconds — that team was made by getting those things wrong and having to fix them. The race condition made the write-lock architecture more robust. The thin artifacts made the regulatory analysis honest. Marcus's memos made Ellen's certification statement a legal instrument rather than a UX feature.

I've watched software teams launch products that didn't deserve to launch. I've watched aviation technology get deployed in contexts where the regulatory analysis was handled by the marketing department. I'm not saying that about Athelon. But I've seen it, and the contrast is what makes February 23rd meaningful.

This is a team that knows what they built. They know the seams. They know what the memos say, because they had to write them. When Carla opens a maintenance record in five years, she's trusting the work-lock logic and the hash function and the re-auth flow — and she can trust it because those things were not designed to impress her. They were designed to survive scrutiny.

## X. Nadia, Later

Nadia Solis is the product manager for Athelon, which means she is the person who has spent two years translating between what the engineers could build, what the regulators required, and what the mechanics would actually use. She was in the office when I called that evening.

"Tell me what you were thinking at 13:51," I said. "When Ellen sat down."

A pause.

"I was thinking about every time we almost shipped something that wasn't ready. There were three moments in this program where we could have gone to a shop with something that looked ready but wasn't. We didn't. We slowed down."

Another pause.

"And then I was thinking: Dave is going to sign that step without thinking about the system. And that's going to mean we built the thing we said we were building."

"Was that what happened?"

"That's what I heard happened." A beat. "I'm going to go watch the audit trail replay in the dashboard tonight. I want to see all twenty-nine events."

## XI. What This Means

I want to be honest about what this moment is and what it isn't.

N4471K is one airplane. Skyline Aviation Services is one shop with five people, in Columbus, Ohio. WO-2026-SAS-0001 is one work order — fourteen task cards, one discrepancy, one part with an 8130-3, one IA sign-off. The world's general aviation maintenance documentation problem is six thousand Part 145 repair stations and a paper-based practice that hasn't materially changed since the 1970s.

One shop is not six thousand shops. One work order is not the whole problem.

But it is the first one.

The hash at the bottom of WO-2026-SAS-0001's maintenance record is a SHA-256 hash of twenty-nine events. If any of those events are altered — if any timestamp is changed, any sign-off edited, any discrepancy disposition modified — the hash will fail. When an FAA Safety Inspector opens that record five years from now, the events that happened in Skyline's middle bay on February 23rd, 2026 will be there in the order they happened, with the proof that they haven't been touched.

The student who flies N4471K next week will not know about any of this. She'll get in the airplane, do her preflight, and fly her instrument cross-country. She will not know that an A&P named Dave spent a Monday morning signing task cards without thinking about them, or that an IA named Ellen sat in front of a tablet and read a certification statement for what she said was the first time in fifteen years, or that a DOM named Carla looked at an eleven-page PDF and, for the first time in twenty years of maintenance work, didn't check it.

She doesn't need to know. That's the point.

The work was done. The record says so. The record is real.

That's not nothing. For GA maintenance documentation in 2026, it is, in fact, quite a lot.
