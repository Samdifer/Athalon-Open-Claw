## 1. Your Identity & Credibility Story

Before you dial a single number, you need a 15-second answer to "Who are you and why should I care?" that doesn't sound like every other cold caller. Aviation shop owners smell generic sales pitches from a mile away.

### The Core Identity (Memorize This)

> "I build websites specifically for FAA Part 145 repair stations. Not restaurants, not dentists, not 'small businesses.' Just aviation maintenance shops. I know the difference between a Class III airframe rating and a limited accessory rating, and I build sites that reflect what your cert actually covers."

**Why this works:** Niche specificity is the #1 Hormozi principle for price power and trust. The moment you say "Part 145" instead of "small business," you've separated yourself from the 10,000 generic web agencies who cold-call shops every week.

### Credibility Anchors (Use 1-2 Per Call, Rotate)

Pick the anchors that are TRUE for you. Do not fabricate credentials.

- **Aviation familiarity:** "I've spent [X months/years] researching the Part 145 world — I know what a PMI looks like, I know why AD tracking matters, and I know the difference between your shop and a Jiffy Lube."
- **Data-driven research:** "I've analyzed every Part 145 repair station in Colorado — all 71 of them. I know which shops show up on Google and which don't. Yours doesn't."
- **Specificity about THEIR shop:** "I pulled your cert from AVInfo. You've got a [cert class] rating for [aircraft types] at [airport]. That's not something I read off a template — I researched your specific operation."
- **FAA cert badge innovation:** "We build an FAA Certificate Verification badge into the homepage — links directly to your live cert record. I came up with that because in this industry, the first thing a customer checks is whether your cert is current. Nobody else in web design is doing this because nobody else knows this industry."

### What NOT to Say

- "I'm a digital marketing agency" — instant hang-up trigger for blue-collar owners
- "I help businesses grow their online presence" — generic, forgettable
- "I do web design and SEO" — positions you as a commodity
- "I'm calling from [Company Name]" as the lead — they don't care about your company name yet

### The 30-Second Bio (For When They Ask "Tell Me About Yourself")

> "Short version — I got into this because I kept seeing the same problem: great aviation shops that are invisible online. Shops doing half-a-million dollars in engine work, but when you Google their specialty, they don't show up. Their competitor in another state gets the call instead. That felt wrong.
>
> So I started building websites specifically for Part 145 shops. Not marketing fluff — sites built around your actual cert capabilities, optimized for the specific searches your customers make. That's all I do."

## 2. Grand Slam Offer Definition

### The Hormozi Value Equation — Applied

```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)
```

**Dream Outcome (maximize):**
> "Your phone rings from aircraft owners who found you on Google — not just the guys who already know you're here."

This is not about having a website. It's about being *findable*. The dream is: customers come to them, instead of them surviving on whoever happens to taxi past their hangar.

**Perceived Likelihood (maximize):**
- Reference specific Google search volume for their niche + market (e.g., "PT6 overhaul Colorado" — actual searches, actual demand)
- Show a before/after from a comparable shop (even outside aviation — auto body shops, machine shops)
- Name their airport and the based-aircraft count: "There are 900 aircraft based at Centennial. How many of those owners know you exist?"

**Time Delay (minimize → zero):**
> "Your site is live in 14 days. Not 3 months. Not 'sometime this quarter.' Fourteen days from today."

**Effort & Sacrifice (minimize → zero):**
> "We build it. You look at it. You say yes or you say change this. That's your total involvement. You don't write copy. You don't pick colors. You don't learn WordPress. You run your shop — we handle the rest."

### The Guarantee

**Type: Conditional (Hormozi-approved — ties guarantee to a measurable outcome, not buyer's feelings)**

> "Here's our guarantee: If your website doesn't appear on the first page of Google for at least one of your target search terms within 90 days of launch, we keep working on it — at no additional cost — until it does. No time limit on that. We don't get to walk away until you're ranking."

**Fine print (state verbally):** "The only condition is that the site stays live and you don't change the hosting or take it down. We can't rank a site that isn't up."

**Why "keep working" beats "full rebuild":** The original version promised a "full rebuild" — that sounds dramatic but is actually a worse deal for both parties. A rebuild resets the clock on SEO. What the customer actually wants is to RANK, not to have a new site. "We keep working until you rank" is a stronger guarantee because it's tied to the outcome they care about, not the deliverable.

**Realistic calibration by archetype:**

| Archetype | 90-Day Ranking Probability | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Rural GA (D) | Very High (90%+) | Near-zero competition for "aircraft maintenance [small town]" — easiest wins |
| Helicopter/Rotorcraft (B) | High (80%) | Niche terms like "AS350 maintenance Colorado" have few competing pages |
| Engine Shop (A) | Moderate-High (70%) | More competition for engine terms but still niche enough |
| Full-Service/Defense (C) | Moderate (55–65%) | Broader terms, more competitors, corporate sites competing — may take 120+ days |

**Honest note:** Do NOT promise this guarantee to Archetype C defense shops (BAE, SNC) — their terms compete with corporate sites with massive domain authority. For large/defense prospects, use a different guarantee: "If you're not satisfied with the site within 30 days of launch, you don't pay the final installment." This is a satisfaction-based conditional guarantee rather than a ranking guarantee.

**Why this works overall:** It's specific (page 1, 90 days, at least one term), it's measurable (both parties can verify), and the risk is on you, not them. Per Hormozi: "The guarantee is not the main driver — it removes the last barrier."

## 3. Target Prospect Segments

### Archetype A — The Invisible Engine Shop

**Profile:** Medium Part 145 shops specializing in engine overhaul, powerplant, or component work. Serious technical capability. Obs score 0.20–0.30. Most business comes from airport proximity and mechanic-to-mechanic referrals.

**Examples from CRM:**
- Aerofield Services (Aurora, KDEN) — obs 0.30, Boeing 737/757/767/777, Airbus A300/A320/A330 engines
- Western Skyways (Montrose, KMTJ) — obs 0.30, Cessna/Piper/Beechcraft piston & turboprop engines
- Precision Aircraft & Engine / Mayo Aviation (Englewood, KAPA) — obs 0.20, engine components

**Core pain:** They do $500K–$2M+ engine jobs but have zero digital footprint. An aircraft owner searching "PT6 overhaul Colorado" finds a shop in Wichita before they find these guys.

**Dream outcome framing:**
> "Imagine an aircraft owner in Aspen needs a hot section on their King Air. They Google it. Right now, they find Dallas Aeronautical or someone in Kansas. What if they found you instead? That's one job. That's a $40,000 invoice from a Google search."

**Emotional hook:** Revenue they're leaving on the table. This isn't about "brand awareness" — it's about invoices.

**Key stat to research pre-call:** Google search volume for "[engine type] overhaul Colorado" or "[aircraft] maintenance [city]."

### Archetype B — The Helicopter/Rotorcraft Specialist

**Profile:** Niche rotorcraft MROs servicing EMS, law enforcement, tour, and utility operators. Obs 0.23–0.30. Tight-knit industry where contracts are relationship-driven — but the first step in every new relationship is a Google search.

**Examples from CRM:**
- Trans Aero LLC (Loveland, KFNL) — obs 0.30, Airbus AS350/H125, Bell 205A1, MD 530F
- Aircam National Helicopter Services (Englewood, KAPA) — obs 0.23, rotorcraft specialty

**Core pain:** EMS directors, flight department managers, and utility operators vet MRO providers online before making a call. A shop with no web presence doesn't make the shortlist — regardless of their actual capability.

**Dream outcome framing:**
> "When an EMS director in Grand Junction is looking for a new MRO provider for their AS350 fleet, the first thing they do is Google. Right now, they find your competitor in Utah. What if the first thing they saw was your shop — your certs, your aircraft types, your location 45 minutes away instead of 6 hours?"

**Emotional hook:** Credibility. In helicopter maintenance, trust is everything. A professional web presence is the digital equivalent of a clean hangar floor.

**Key stat to research pre-call:** Number of EMS/law enforcement helicopter bases within 200nm of their shop.

**Emotional hook:** Competitive parity. They're losing to shops that aren't better — just better marketed.

**Key stat to research pre-call:** Their competitor's website quality. Find a direct competitor with a polished site and reference the contrast.

## 4. Master Cold Call Script (CLOSER Framework)

### Pre-Call Preparation (30 seconds)

Before dialing, confirm:
- [ ] Prospect name, title if known (DOM, owner, manager)
- [ ] Shop name and airport (ICAO code)
- [ ] Cert class and aircraft types from CRM data
- [ ] Current website status (has one? quality? last updated?)
- [ ] Archetype assignment (A/B/C/D) and corresponding dream outcome framing
- [ ] One personalized detail (aircraft type, recent cert change, airport-specific fact)

---

### Opening — The ANOT Frame + Pattern Interrupt

**[Ring... answer]**

> **You:** "[First name]? Hey — this is [Your Name]. I'm calling about your shop out at [Airport Name]. Do you have about 90 seconds, or did I catch you under a cowling?"

**Why this works:**
- Uses their first name (familiarity)
- Names their airport (proves you did homework — you're not reading from a generic list)
- "90 seconds" is a low commitment ask
- "Under a cowling" is aviation-native humor — signals you understand their world. A non-aviation caller would never say this. **Calibration note:** Only use this if you can deliver it naturally. If you're not comfortable with aviation humor, swap it for: "or did I catch you at a bad time?" The worst thing is aviation slang that sounds rehearsed — shop owners will clock it instantly and trust drops. Better to be genuine and non-aviation than fake and aviation-fluent.

**If they say "Who is this?" / "What's this about?":**

> **You:** "Fair enough — I'm [Your Name], I work with Part 145 shops on their web presence. I was looking at your FAA cert listing and noticed your shop does [specific capability — e.g., 'Airbus rotorcraft work' or 'Class III airframe and powerplant'] out at [Airport]. I had a quick question about how your customers typically find you. Is now okay or should I call back at a better time?"

**If they say "Now's not a good time":**

> **You:** "Totally get it — when's a better window? I'll call you back at exactly that time. Morning or afternoon work better for you?"

*Book it. Call back exactly when you said you would. Reliability is a trust signal.*

---

### C — Clarify

**Goal:** Understand how their customers currently find them. Uncover whether they've thought about their web presence before.

> **You:** "So here's why I called — I was looking at Part 145 shops in Colorado, and I came across your cert listing. You're running a [cert class / specialty] shop at [Airport], working on [aircraft types]. Solid operation.
>
> But when I Googled your shop name, [describe what you actually found — e.g., 'I found a basic site that hasn't been updated in a while' or 'I couldn't find a website at all' or 'your Google Business listing is pretty bare'].
>
> I'm curious — how do most of your customers find you right now?"

**Listen. Let them talk. Take notes.**

Common answers and what they reveal:
- *"Word of mouth"* → They know they're dependent on referrals but may not see it as a problem yet
- *"We're at the airport, people just walk in"* → Geographic dependency, vulnerable to any shop that opens nearby
- *"We've been here 30 years, everyone knows us"* → Legacy confidence; the owner who retires or the mechanic who leaves takes the rolodex with them
- *"We get some calls from our website"* → They have one but it's underperforming; quantify it — "How many per month? What would double that be worth?"

**Follow-up probes:**

> "When you get a new customer — someone who's never used your shop before — what's the typical story? How'd they hear about you?"
>
> "Have you ever lost a job to a shop that you know isn't as good as yours? What happened there?"
>
> "If I'm an aircraft owner in [nearby city] and I need [their specialty], and I Google it — what do I find?"

---

### L — Label the Problem

**Goal:** Get them to agree, out loud, on the core problem. This is the gating step — do not proceed until they say "yes, that's it."

> **You:** "So if I'm hearing you right — you've got a solid operation, you do quality work, your customers are happy. But practically all of your business comes from people who already know you're there. If someone new moves to the area, or a pilot is transiting through [airport], or an aircraft owner is shopping for a [service] provider online — they're probably not finding you. Is that a fair way to put it?"

**Wait for confirmation.** If they push back ("We're fine, we're busy"), don't argue:

> "I hear that. Let me ask it differently — if you could add 2 or 3 new customers per month without lifting a finger, without a single cold call or networking event — just from people finding you online — would that move the needle for your shop?"

**The label you're landing on:**

> "So the gap isn't your work — it's your visibility. The people who know you, love you. The problem is the people who don't know you yet."

---

### O — Overview Past Attempts

**Goal:** Surface what they've tried before and why it didn't work. This builds the "circle of pain" that makes your solution feel different.

> **You:** "This probably isn't the first time someone's brought up your web presence. Have you tried anything before? A website, a Facebook page, a listing somewhere?"

**Common responses and follow-ups:**

**"Yeah, we have a website. My [nephew/friend/office manager] built it."**
> "How's that working for you? Are you getting calls from it, or is it more of a digital business card that's just... there?"

**"We paid someone to build a site years ago and it didn't do anything."**
> "What happened? Did it just not show up in searches? Did it look like your shop, or was it kind of generic? ... That's actually one of the most common things I hear. The site gets built, nobody optimizes it for search, and it just sits there. That's not a website problem — that's a strategy problem."

**"No, we've never really done anything."**
> "What's kept you from it? Time? Not knowing where to start? Had a bad experience with a web company before?"

**"We're on Facebook."**
> "That's good for staying in touch with people who already follow you. The gap is — when someone Googles '[aircraft type] maintenance [city],' Facebook doesn't show up. Google shows websites. And right now, the websites it shows are your competitors'."

**Key principle:** Don't trash what they've done. Diagnose why it didn't produce results. Position yourself as the person who understands why the last attempt failed.

---

### S — Sell the Vacation (NOT the Flight)

**Goal:** Paint the destination — what their business looks like AFTER the problem is solved. No features. No "we use WordPress" or "we do SEO." Just the outcome.

> **You:** "Here's what I'd want for your shop.
>
> You come into work on a Monday morning. You check your phone — there's a voicemail from an aircraft owner in [nearby city] who found you on Google last night. He's got a [aircraft type relevant to their cert] that needs [service relevant to their specialty]. He already looked at your site, he already saw your certs, he already read about your shop. He's not price shopping — he's calling because he picked you.
>
> That's one call. Now imagine that happens twice a month. Three times. Even at the low end, that's an extra $50K–$100K a year in revenue from people who found you, not people you chased.
>
> That's what a real web presence does for a shop like yours."

**Do NOT explain HOW you build the site.** Do not mention technology, platforms, SEO acronyms, or design processes. They don't care yet. They need to want the destination before they'll tolerate hearing about the journey.

**Archetype-specific vacation paintings are in Section 4.**

---

### E — Explain Away Concerns (Pre-Emptive)

**Goal:** Surface and dissolve objections BEFORE asking for commitment. You name the concern so they don't have to.

> **You:** "Now — most shop owners I talk to at this point have a couple of things going through their head, so let me address them.
>
> First one is time. You're busy. You don't have time to sit in meetings about fonts and colors. I get it. Here's how this works: we do the work. We build the site based on your cert data, your aircraft types, your location. We show it to you. You say 'looks good' or 'change this.' That's it. Your total time investment is about 30 minutes.
>
> Second one is cost. Before I give you a number, let me ask you this — what's one new engine job worth to your shop? [Wait for answer.] Okay — so if this site brings you even one extra job in the first 6 months, it's paid for itself several times over. This isn't an expense. It's a customer acquisition tool with a measurable return.
>
> Third one is trust. You've either been burned by a web company before, or you know someone who has. Generic sites, broken promises, they disappear after they cash the check. That's why we don't take final payment until your site is live and you've approved it. And that's why we guarantee first-page Google ranking within 90 days — or we rebuild it, on us."

---

### R — Reinforce the Decision + Close for Next Step

**Goal:** Book a 15-minute screen-share demo or in-person meeting. NOT a sale. The call goal is the next step.

> **You:** "Look — I'm not asking you to sign anything today. What I'd like to do is show you what this looks like for a shop like yours. I'll pull up an example, walk you through the offer, and you can decide if it makes sense. Takes 15 minutes.
>
> I've got [Day 1] at [time] and [Day 2] at [time]. Which works better?"

**If they agree:** Book it immediately. Confirm their email. Send a calendar invite before you hang up.

> "Perfect. I'll send you a calendar invite right now — you'll get it in the next 5 minutes. And I'll include a one-pager on what we do so you can take a look before we meet. Talk to you [day]."

**If they want to think about it:**

> "Totally fair. Before I let you go — what's the one thing you'd want to see to know if this is worth your time? That way when we do connect, I can show you exactly that."

---

### When the Call Goes Off-Script (Transition Bridges)

Real calls don't follow a linear path. The prospect will jump ahead, circle back, or go on tangents. Here's how to get back on track without being awkward.

**They jump straight to "How much does this cost?" before you've even pitched:**

> "I'll give you the full breakdown — but before I do, let me make sure this is even relevant for your shop. Otherwise I'm wasting both our time. Can I ask you a couple quick questions first?"

*[Route back to C — Clarify.]*

**They tell you their whole life story during C (Clarify) and you've been listening for 5 minutes:**

> "Man — I appreciate you sharing all that. It sounds like you've got a real operation going. Let me boil it down to make sure I've got it right..."

*[Route to L — Label. Summarize what you heard into the core problem.]*

**They say "yeah, that's basically it" during L (Label) but seem lukewarm:**

> "Got it. And I'm guessing this isn't the first time someone's raised this with you. Have you ever tried to do anything about it?"

*[Route to O — Overview. Their past failures will energize the conversation more than your pitch will.]*

**They're clearly interested during S (Vacation) and say "Okay, so how does this work?":**

> "Let me hit the main questions most shop owners have at this point, and then I'll walk you through the next step."

*[Route to E — Explain. Don't skip it just because they're eager. The pre-emptive objection handling prevents buyer's remorse later.]*

**They raise an objection mid-pitch (any step):**

> "That's a fair point — let me address that right now."

*[Handle it with AAA from Section 6. Then: "Does that make sense? ... Good. So here's what I was saying..."]*

*[Return to wherever you were in the CLOSER sequence.]*

**The call is going great but you're running out of time:**

> "I want to be respectful of your time — I know I said 90 seconds and we're at about [X] minutes. Can I have 2 more minutes to show you what the next step looks like?"

*[If yes — jump directly to R (Reinforce/Close for demo). Skip anything you haven't covered and save it for the demo.]*

**Use for:** Aerofield Services, Western Skyways, Precision Aircraft/Mayo Aviation, Phoenix Turbine Overhaul

**S — Sell the Vacation:**

## 6. Objection Handlers (AAA Framework)

Every objection uses the Hormozi AAA structure:
- **Acknowledge** — Mirror, don't argue. Show you heard them.
- **Associate** — Link their concern to successful buyer behavior. Reframe resistance as thoughtfulness.
- **Ask** — A question that re-opens the conversation or reveals the real concern.

---

### Objection 1: "We don't need a website — all our business is word-of-mouth"

**Root fear:** Change is unnecessary. What they have works.

> **Acknowledge:** "I hear you — and honestly, that tells me you do great work. You don't keep customers 30 years by being mediocre. Word-of-mouth is the best marketing there is."
>
> **Associate:** "The shops I work with that say the same thing — they're usually right. Their existing customers love them. The question they eventually ask themselves is: what happens when my best referral source retires? What happens when a new owner buys a hangar at my airport and Googles 'aircraft maintenance near me' instead of asking the line guy? That's the customer you'll never know you lost."
>
> **Ask:** "Let me ask you this — in the last year, how many new customers came to you from something other than a personal referral? ... And what would it be worth to add 2 or 3 more per month without doing anything differently?"

---

### Objection 2: "We already have a website"

**Root fear:** They've checked this box. Someone already handled it.

> **Acknowledge:** "Good — you're ahead of most shops I talk to. That's actually a great starting point."
>
> **Associate:** "The thing I see with a lot of aviation shops is — they have a site, but it's more of a digital business card than a customer acquisition tool. It's got your name, your address, maybe a stock photo of an airplane. But it's not optimized for search, it's not built around your cert capabilities, and it's not converting visitors into phone calls. The shops I work with that had an existing site and upgraded — they tell me the difference was like going from a hangar with the doors closed to one with a neon sign on the highway."
>
> **Ask:** "How many calls per month do you get directly from your website? ... What would it look like if that number doubled?"

---

### Objection 3: "How much does this cost?"

**Root fear:** Financial risk. Getting ripped off. Spending money on something that doesn't work.

**Do not lead with price. Lead with the cost of the problem.**

> **Acknowledge:** "Fair question — and I respect that you want to know what you're getting into. Let me give you the honest answer."
>
> **Associate:** "The shops I work with that asked the same question — here's what they figured out. They looked at what one new customer is worth to them. Not a one-time oil change — a customer who comes back for annuals, for squawk repairs, for the pre-buy when they sell the airplane. Over 3 years, most shops tell me that's $10K to $50K per customer depending on the aircraft. The ones who went ahead and did this told me it paid for itself off the first Google lead."
>
> **Ask:** "Let me ask you this — what's one new customer worth to your shop over 3 years? ... [Wait for their number.] Okay. So if this site brings you even one new customer this year — just one — does the math work?"
>
> *[If they say yes — state the price. If they hesitate — continue:]*
>
> "And here's the other piece — we don't take final payment until your site is live and you've approved it. You see exactly what you're getting before you pay for it. Does that change the equation?"

**Note:** Always end this exchange with a question. The moment you make a declarative statement ("This is the cheapest salesperson you'll ever hire"), you've stopped selling and started lecturing. The question keeps them engaged and gets them to convince themselves.

---

### Objection 4: "I'm too busy to deal with this right now"

**Root fear:** Overwhelm. One more thing on the pile.

> **Acknowledge:** "I believe you. Shop owners aren't sitting around with empty calendars. You're the DOM, the accountant, and the parts runner all in one."
>
> **Associate:** "Here's what I've found though — the shops that are busiest are the ones who benefit the most from this. Because when you're slammed, you're not networking. You're not making calls. You're not going to trade shows. So where do your next customers come from when your current referral pipeline dries up? The shops I work with that said 'I'm too busy' and then did it anyway — they all say the same thing: 'I wish I'd done this during my last slow season instead of waiting until I was desperate for new work.'"
>
> **Ask:** "I'm not asking for your time. We build it. You look at it for 20 minutes and tell us if it's right. That's it. Can I show you an example so you can decide if it's even worth those 20 minutes? I'll be quick — I respect that you're busy."

---

### Objection 5: "I need to talk to my partner / co-owner"

**Root fear:** Making a decision alone. Accountability.

> **Acknowledge:** "Of course. That's the responsible move — I'd do the same thing."
>
> **Associate:** "The partners and co-owners I've talked to usually have one of two questions: 'What does it cost?' and 'How do we know it'll work?' Let me make sure you can answer both of those."
>
> **Coach the champion:**
> "What do you think [partner name] will push back on — the cost or the concept? ... Okay. Here's what I'd tell them: [address their specific likely concern]. And here — let me send you a one-pager you can forward. It's got the offer, the guarantee, and an example of what the site looks like. That way they're seeing real information, not a secondhand pitch."
>
> **Ask:** "Would it make sense for all three of us to jump on a 15-minute call? That way I can answer their questions directly and you don't have to play telephone."

---

### Objection 6: "We tried a website before and it didn't do anything"

**Root fear:** History repeating. Trust destroyed by a prior vendor.

This is the most important objection to handle well. If you nail this one, you win the deal.

> **Acknowledge:** "That's actually the most common thing I hear from shop owners. And honestly — it's usually justified. Most web companies build a pretty site, hand over the keys, and disappear. Nobody optimizes it for search. Nobody makes sure Google can find it. It just sits there."
>
> **Associate:** "The difference between a site that 'doesn't do anything' and a site that generates phone calls is usually one thing: search optimization. Your last site was probably fine as a brochure. But nobody told Google it existed. Nobody built it around the specific terms your customers search for. Nobody set up your Google Business Profile to point to it.
>
> "The shops I work with that had the same experience and gave it another shot — what they tell me is: 'Oh, that's what it was supposed to feel like.' Because now the phone actually rings from it."
>
> **Ask:** "Here's what I'll do — let me pull up your old site right now. I'll tell you in 60 seconds exactly why it wasn't working. No charge, no obligation. If my diagnosis doesn't make sense, you hang up and I never call again. Fair?"

**This is powerful because:** You're offering to solve a mystery for free. You're diagnosing their past pain. And it transitions naturally into "Here's what we'd do differently."

## 7. Gatekeeper Scripts

Most shops have someone answering the phone who isn't the owner or DOM — an office manager, a receptionist, or a lead tech who happened to pick up. You have about 10 seconds before they decide whether to transfer you or brush you off.

### Rule #1: Do NOT Pitch the Gatekeeper

They cannot buy from you. Save the value proposition for the decision-maker. Your only goal is to get transferred or get a callback time.

### Script A — Direct and Respectful

> **Gatekeeper:** "[Shop Name], how can I help you?"
>
> **You:** "Hey — this is [Your Name]. I was trying to reach [Owner Name / the shop owner / whoever handles your marketing]. Is he around?"

**If they ask "What's this regarding?":**

> "I was looking at your FAA cert listing and had a quick question about your shop's web presence. It'll take about 2 minutes. Is he available, or is there a better time to catch him?"

**Key moves:**
- Use the owner's first name if you have it (from CRM data) — signals familiarity
- "FAA cert listing" creates curiosity and sounds official without being deceptive
- Never pitch. Never explain the full offer. "A quick question about your web presence" is enough.

### Script B — When They Won't Transfer

> **Gatekeeper:** "Can I take a message?"
>
> **You:** "Sure — tell him [Your Name] called about the shop's web presence. I was doing some research on Part 145 shops in Colorado and had something I wanted to run by him. My number is [number]. But honestly, the best thing is if I just call back — when's he usually free?"

**Why this works:** You're asking for their routine, not demanding access. Most will tell you "He's usually here mornings" or "Try after 2 PM." Now you have a window.

### Script C — The Office Manager Who Screens Everything

Some small shops have an office manager who considers it their job to reject all sales calls. Do not fight this. Recruit them.

> **Gatekeeper:** "We're not interested in any marketing services."
>
> **You:** "Totally understand — and I'm not a marketing agency. I specifically work with FAA Part 145 repair stations on their web presence. I noticed [Shop Name] doesn't show up when you Google [their specialty] in [their city]. I just wanted to let [Owner] know — it's not a pitch, it's more of a heads-up. Can I leave my number in case he's curious?"

**The reframe:** You're not selling — you're giving them information. This often gets the office manager to at least pass along the message, because now THEY'RE curious about the Google thing.

## 12. Disqualification Criteria

Not every prospect on the list is a real opportunity. Hormozi is clear: time spent on unqualified prospects is time stolen from qualified ones. Here's when to STOP selling and move on.

### Hard Disqualifiers (Walk Away Immediately)

| Signal | Why | Action |
|---|---|---|
| They tell you they're closing/selling the shop | No long-term ROI | Thank them, remove from list |
| The "shop" is actually one person working out of a garage | Can't afford the investment, and the ROI math doesn't work | Politely decline, offer a free resource |
| They're hostile or abusive on the call | Life's too short | "I appreciate your time. Take care." Hang up. |
| The cert is inactive or expired | No business to drive traffic to | Remove from list |

### Soft Disqualifiers (Nurture, Don't Close)

| Signal | Why | Action |
|---|---|---|
| BANT score under 7 (no budget, no authority, vague need, 6+ month timeline) | Not ready yet | Move to monthly nurture email, check back in 90 days |
| They're mid-move or mid-expansion | Bad timing, not bad fit | Book a follow-up for when they're settled |
| The decision-maker is unreachable after 5+ attempts | Can't sell to someone who won't talk to you | Move to long-term nurture, try LinkedIn |

### Enterprise / Defense Contractor Reality Check

**BAE Systems, Sierra Nevada Company, GoGo Business Aviation, Pilatus Aircraft USA** — these are NOT typical website sales. They are billion-dollar companies with corporate marketing departments and brand guidelines. The pitch for these prospects is fundamentally different:

- **Do NOT pitch a full website rebuild.** Their corporate sites are controlled by HQ.
- **DO pitch a dedicated landing page or microsite** for their local Part 145 operation that sits alongside (not replaces) the corporate site.
- **DO pitch Google Business Profile optimization** — this is where the local opportunity lives, and corporate marketing often ignores local listings.
- **The decision-maker is NOT the owner.** It's a facility manager, station chief, or regional ops director. You'll need to navigate corporate org charts.
- **The budget process is different.** They have money but require purchase orders, vendor approvals, and multi-layer sign-offs. Be patient. Sales cycle: 60-120 days minimum.
- **The value proposition is different.** Not "get more customers" (they get customers through corporate channels). It's "make your local operation findable for walk-in AOG requests, transient aircraft, and regional operators who don't know you have a local facility."

If you're early in your sales career, **skip the enterprise prospects.** Focus on the independent and small-to-medium shops where you can close in 1-2 calls. Come back to the big fish when you have case studies and confidence.

## 14. Appendix: Quick-Reference Cheat Sheet

### The Hormozi Value Equation (Tape This to Your Monitor)

```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)

MAXIMIZE the top:
  → Dream Outcome: "Your phone rings from Google"
  → Perceived Likelihood: Show proof from similar shops

MINIMIZE the bottom:
  → Time Delay: "Live in 14 days"
  → Effort: "You approve it. That's it."
```

### CLOSER Steps (Never Skip, Never Reorder)

| Step | One-Line Reminder |
|---|---|
| **C** — Clarify | "How do your customers find you right now?" |
| **L** — Label | "So the gap isn't your work — it's your visibility." |
| **O** — Overview | "What have you tried before? How'd that go?" |
| **S** — Sell the Vacation | Paint the Monday morning phone call |
| **E** — Explain | Pre-empt time, cost, and trust concerns |
| **R** — Reinforce | Book the 15-minute demo |

### AAA for Every Objection (Never Argue)

| Step | What to Do |
|---|---|
| **Acknowledge** | Mirror it. "I hear you." |
| **Associate** | Link to successful buyers. "The best shops I work with said the same thing." |
| **Ask** | Re-open. "What would make this a no-brainer?" |

### Call Metrics to Track

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Dials per day | 25–40 |
| Conversations (live answers) | 8–12 |
| Voicemails left | 15–25 |
| Demos booked per day | 1–2 |
| Show rate (demos that happen) | ≥70% |
| Close rate (demos → sold) | ≥40% |

### Daily Call Block Playbook

Calls are best made Tuesday through Thursday, 9:30–11:30 AM and 2:00–4:00 PM Mountain Time. Mondays are hectic (catching up from weekend), Fridays are mentally checked out.

**Pre-Block (15 min):**
1. Pull up today's call list (5–10 prospects)
2. Review each pre-call intel brief — 30 seconds per prospect
3. Open their website (or Google results showing they don't have one) in separate tabs
4. Have the CLOSER cheat sheet visible

**Call Block (90 min):**
1. Dial in priority order: Tier B (have contact) before Tier C (need enrichment)
2. If you get voicemail → leave VM script → send text → send Email #1. Move to next dial. Total time: 3 minutes.
3. If you get a gatekeeper → use gatekeeper script → note callback time. Move to next dial. Total time: 2 minutes.
4. If you get the decision-maker → run CLOSER script. Aim for 8–12 minutes. Book the demo or note the follow-up action.
5. After each live conversation, spend 2 minutes logging notes: what they said, which objections came up, next step, BANT score.

**Post-Block (15 min):**
1. Send all calendar invites for booked demos
2. Send personalized follow-up emails for live conversations
3. Update CRM with notes and next actions
4. Count: dials, conversations, VMs, demos booked

**Weekly Target:**
- 25–40 dials per call block × 3 blocks per week = 75–120 dials/week
- Expect 25–35% live answer rate = 20–40 conversations/week
- Expect 10–15% demo booking rate from conversations = 2–6 demos/week
- At 40% close rate = 1–2 new clients/week

### Common Mistakes (Learn From Others' Failures)

| Mistake | What Happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Pitching before Labeling | They feel "sold to" not "understood" | Do not move to S until they've said "yes, that's my problem" in L |
| Reading the script word-for-word | You sound robotic, they tune out | Memorize the INTENT of each step, not the exact words |
| Talking more than 40% of the call | You learn nothing, they feel bulldozed | After every question, wait 3 full seconds. They'll fill the silence. |
| Skipping O (Overview/Past Attempts) | You miss their biggest objection before it surfaces | Their past failures are the #1 key to your sale. Never skip this. |
| Handling objections with facts instead of AAA | They feel argued with, not heard | Always Acknowledge first. Facts come after they feel understood. |
| Not booking the demo with a specific time | "I'll send you some info" = they ghost | Always offer two specific time slots. "Tuesday at 10 or Thursday at 2?" |
| Following up once and giving up | 80% of sales happen after the 5th contact | Run the full cadence (call, VM, text, 5 emails). Most won't buy until touch 4-7. |
| Selling on the cold call instead of booking the demo | Too much too soon, they feel pressured | The cold call goal is a 15-minute demo. Period. Don't try to close on the first call. |
